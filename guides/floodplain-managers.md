# Elevation Certificate Guide for Floodplain Managers

Technical guidance for local floodplain management officials on EC requirements, validation, documentation, and community responsibilities.

## Community Responsibilities for Elevation Certificate Accuracy

As a floodplain management official, you are responsible for verifying the accuracy and completeness of Elevation Certificates submitted for your community. The accuracy of your community's EC file directly affects:

- Individual insurance ratings (errors can over- or under-charge property owners)
- Floodplain management compliance documentation
- Map update requests (LOMA/LOMR-F)
- Reconstruction and substantial improvement verification
- Community Rating System (CRS) participation
- Your community's credibility with FEMA and insurers

### Your Core Responsibilities

1. **Initial Review:** Verify EC completeness and consistency when submitted
2. **Technical Accuracy:** Check elevation calculations and datum conversions
3. **Documentation:** Maintain organized EC files accessible for future reference
4. **Error Resolution:** Develop processes to identify and correct EC errors
5. **Professional Communication:** Work with surveyors/engineers on corrections and improvements

## Elevation Certificate Quality Review Process

### Step 1: Completeness Check

When an EC is submitted, verify all required sections are present and signed:

**Required Sections**
- Section A: Building location and characteristics (complete address, parcel number, flood zone, BFE)
- Section B: Building description with diagram showing elevation reference points
- Section C: Vertical datum and reference information
- Section D: Elevation information with professional certification and license
- Section E: Equipment and machinery elevations
- Section F: Flood opening documentation (if applicable)
- Section G: Surveyor/engineer comments and certification statement

**Professional Certification**
- Original ink signature or certified reproduction
- Professional license number (verifiable in your state)
- Professional seal or stamp (if required by state)
- Certification date and statement

**Incomplete EC?**
- Return to professional immediately for completion
- Do not file incomplete documents
- Do not use for underwriting or rating decisions until complete

### Step 2: Data Consistency Review

Compare all sections to ensure internal consistency:

**Section B ↔ Section D Consistency:**
- Building diagram shows lowest floor location?
- Diagram identifies grade reference point(s)?
- Lowest floor elevation documented in Section D?
- Description of foundation type matches diagram?

**Section D ↔ Section E Consistency:**
- Equipment elevations documented?
- Lowest equipment elevation clearly identified?
- All critical equipment listed: furnace, water heater, heat pump, A/C, elevator (if present)?
- Equipment location (inside/outside building footprint) documented?

**Section C ↔ Section D Consistency:**
- Vertical datum documented in Section C?
- Same datum used in elevation measurements?
- If datum conversion needed, is factor documented?
- BFE datum matches current FIRM datum?

**Diagram ↔ Measurements Consistency:**
- Are measurement reference points shown in diagram?
- Do measurements in Section D originate from points shown in diagram?
- Is direction of measurement clear (measuring to lowest adjacent grade)?

**Red Flag Examples:**
- Diagram shows elevation from one point; narrative describes different point
- FFH documented but no diagram or methodology shown
- Equipment elevation higher than building's lowest floor (impossible)
- Datum conversion not documented when using older datum
- Section G notes "unable to access basement" but Section D documents basement elevation

### Step 3: Technical Accuracy Review

Verify elevation calculations are mathematically correct:

**Calculation Check:**
FFH = Lowest building elevation – Lowest adjacent grade elevation

Example:
- Building lowest floor at 125.5' NAVD 1988
- Lowest adjacent grade at 118.2' NAVD 1988
- FFH = 125.5 – 118.2 = 7.3 feet ✓

**Elevation Relative to BFE:**
Building elevation vs. BFE = Building elevation – BFE elevation

Example:
- Building lowest floor: 125.5' NAVD 1988
- BFE: 120.0' NAVD 1988
- Building is 5.5' above BFE ✓

**Datum Conversion (if applicable):**
If EC uses NGVD 1929 but FIRM uses NAVD 1988:
- Verify conversion factor is documented
- Check conversion is correctly applied
- Typical conversion range: +1 to +3 feet depending on location
- FEMA provides conversion factors by county

Example:
- NGVD elevation: 122.4'
- Conversion factor: +2.1' (documented in EC)
- NAVD equivalent: 124.5' ✓

**Reasonableness Check:**
- For pre-FIRM buildings in AE zones, is FFH relatively low (at or near grade)?
- For modern elevated buildings in A zones, is FFH 5+ feet?
- For V Zone buildings, are elevations well above BFE (typically 10+ feet)?
- Do elevations match building appearance (you can verify on inspection)?

### Step 4: Professional Competency Review

Verify the professional who completed the EC is qualified:

**License Verification:**
- License number is valid for your state
- License is current (not expired)
- Discipline appropriate (surveyor for survey; engineer if engineering involved)
- Contact state licensing board if you cannot verify

**Scope of Practice:**
- Surveying: Horizontal and vertical positioning, boundary surveys, elevation documentation
- Engineering: Structural, civil, or PE license appropriate for the work
- Check your state's requirements—some states restrict who can complete ECs

**Professional Standards:**
- Does the EC reflect professional standards (NSPS, ASCE)?
- Are methodology and measurements documented?
- Are assumptions and limitations clearly stated?
- Is certification statement appropriate for scope of work?

**Red Flags:**
- Professional not licensed in your state
- License expired
- Professional discipline not appropriate for type of work
- No professional seal or signature
- Signature is photocopy or appears altered

### Step 5: Site Condition Documentation

Verify field methodology and site documentation:

**Photographs:**
- Are photographs included showing building elevation?
- Do photos show grade reference point?
- Are utility/equipment locations documented?
- Can you verify building elevation from photos (visual sense check)?

**Site Conditions:**
- Are significant site conditions documented (slope, wetlands, adjacent structures, etc.)?
- Is grade variation across the site noted?
- Are limitations or uncertainties documented in Section G?
- For crawlspaces, are access limitations documented?

**Methodology:**
- Is the measurement methodology described (survey, GPS, laser, tape measure)?
- Equipment used identified (transit, GPS unit, laser level)?
- Number of reference points used?
- Is vertical control adequate (tied to reliable benchmarks)?

## Options When Elevation Certificate Has Errors

You will inevitably receive ECs with errors. Develop a documented process:

### Minor Error: Clearly Correctable

**Definition:** Typographical error, wrong flood zone, or math error, but underlying data is solid.

**Process:**
1. Return to surveyor/engineer for confirmation of error
2. Request written correction on letterhead with professional signature
3. Attach correction letter to original EC in your file
4. Reference correction in your internal documentation
5. Use corrected data for rating/underwriting

**Example:** EC shows flood zone AE; current FIRM shows zone X. Professional confirms the building moved out of zone due to map update. Attached correction letter documents this.

### Major Data Issue: Return for Re-Certification

**Definition:** Inconsistent sections, datum mismatch not documented, significant elevation discrepancy, or incomplete field work.

**Process:**
1. Document the specific issue in writing
2. Return EC to professional with detailed explanation
3. Request either:
   - Corrected EC with re-measurement, OR
   - Written explanation of discrepancy
4. Do not file or use until resolved
5. Consider whether different professional should complete new EC

**Example:** EC documents FFH as 12 feet but Section G notes "unable to access crawlspace." Without knowing crawlspace elevation, FFH calculation is unreliable. Request either access and re-measurement or explanation of how FFH was determined.

### Unresolvable Error: Document and Note in File

**Definition:** Error cannot be resolved, or professional will not respond.

**Process:**
1. Attach memo to EC file documenting the issue
2. Note the date you identified the problem
3. Document attempts to contact professional
4. Note that EC is accepted "subject to" the documented concern
5. Flag for future review or re-certification requirement
6. Consider requesting updated EC from different professional

**Example:** EC contains mathematical error in FFH calculation. Professional does not respond to correction request. You attach memo noting the error, the expected correct value, and that EC is accepted pending future verification.

### Using Section G for Documentation

The surveyor/engineer's comments section (Section G) is your opportunity to flag issues:

**Add notes such as:**
- "Reviewed and accepted with minor corrections (see attached memo dated [date])"
- "Subject to confirmation of crawlspace elevation—professional unable to access"
- "Accepted pending verification of equipment location"
- "Datum conversion verified against FEMA factor for [county]"
- "Building diagram reference points verified against field observation"

**Sign and date your notation.** This creates an audit trail showing you reviewed the document and documented your concerns.

## Pre-Populating Sections A and B at Permit Application

Efficiency improvement: You can pre-populate some EC sections when issuing permits for construction in flood-prone areas.

### What You Can Pre-Populate

**Section A:** Building location information
- Property address
- Parcel number
- Community and county
- Current flood zone
- Base Flood Elevation (from current FIRM)
- Vertical datum (from FIRM)

**Section B Diagram:** Building location on community map or site plan

### How to Implement

1. **Create Template:** Develop a form or printable EC with your community data pre-filled
2. **Distribute at Permit:** Provide to applicant when issuing floodplain development permit
3. **Instructions:** Provide clear instructions that applicant must provide to surveyor/engineer
4. **Your Stamp:** Stamp with your community seal to indicate pre-filled sections are official

### Benefits

- Reduces errors in Section A (typos, wrong BFE, unclear zone)
- Ensures consistent, official BFE and datum information
- Speeds up surveyor/engineer work
- Improves compliance with permit conditions

## Inspection Schedule: Three-Point Process

Develop a consistent inspection schedule tied to EC requirements:

### Inspection 1: Pre-Construction

**Purpose:** Verify lowest floor elevation will be above BFE.

**Timing:** Before construction begins (after permit approval).

**What to Verify:**
- Ground elevation at building location
- Stakes or marks showing proposed lowest floor elevation
- Grade reference points for measuring FFH
- Site conditions affecting drainage or flooding

**Documentation:**
- Mark site plan showing grade measurements
- Photograph showing grade reference points
- Verify FFH will meet requirements

**If Non-Compliant:**
- Stop work
- Require design modification
- Require surveyor consultation

### Inspection 2: Pre-Lowest Floor

**Purpose:** Verify lowest floor elevation during construction, before finishes/equipment installed.

**Timing:** After building structure is substantially complete but before flooring/mechanical systems installed.

**What to Verify:**
- Building dimensions and footprint match plans
- Lowest floor height above identified grade
- Grade reference point(s) established
- Basement or crawlspace dimensions and elevation
- Equipment/utility locations for planned elevation

**Documentation:**
- Photograph lowest floor elevation relative to grade
- Mark grade reference points
- Record measurements
- Note any variations from design

**If Non-Compliant:**
- Work with builder/surveyor to correct before finishes
- May require fill, piers, or structural modification
- Document resolution

### Inspection 3: Final (As-Built)

**Purpose:** Verify completed building elevation and that EC accurately reflects as-built conditions.

**Timing:** After construction complete, before occupancy permit or final approval.

**What to Verify:**
- Building's actual lowest floor elevation matches EC
- Photograph the completed structure and lowest floor
- Verify equipment elevations match Section E
- Confirm flood openings (if required) are installed and functional
- Verify final grade matches elevation reference point used for FFH

**Documentation:**
- Compare EC measurements to your final inspection observations
- Photograph showing final conditions
- Mark any discrepancies
- Verify utilities are properly elevated

**If Non-Compliant:**
- Do not issue occupancy permit until corrected
- May require updated EC or structural modification
- Verify corrected EC before final approval

### Inspection Coordination with EC Professional

After final inspection:

1. If you observe discrepancies between your measurements and EC, notify surveyor/engineer
2. Request explanation or correction
3. Verify EC accuracy before filing
4. Document any corrections required

## Section G Completion Guidance

Section G is where the surveyor/engineer documents observations, limitations, and assumptions.

### As Floodplain Manager, Review for:

**Scope of Services:**
- "Measured building elevation from grade to lowest floor"
- "Documented equipment location and elevation"
- "Verified flood opening dimensions and location"
- Clear statement of what was done

**Limitations:**
- "Crawlspace not accessed due to blocked opening" (may require note)
- "Equipment location estimated based on visual inspection" (may warrant verification)
- "Grade elevation estimated from adjacent grade" (less precise)
- "Building under construction—subject to verification upon completion"

**Significant Findings:**
- "Building appears to have been elevated since original construction"
- "Apparent evidence of previous flooding in basement"
- "Grade slopes significantly; see diagram for reference points"

### Red Flags in Section G

- "Unable to determine" lowest floor elevation (return for re-work)
- "Relied on builder/owner information" without verification (problematic)
- "Measurements estimated" when precision required (return for actual survey)
- No comment at all (may suggest insufficient effort)

### Your Additions to Section G

You can add notes such as:
- "Reviewed by [your name, title] on [date]; verified against final inspection"
- "Subject to correction pending [specific verification]"
- "Accepted with attached correction memo"
- "Original elevation used for as-built verification; confirmed satisfactory"

Sign and date your note.

## Documenting As-Built Lowest Floor Determination

When verifying final building elevation, use systematic documentation:

### Figure 1 Guidance (Typical Documentation)

Your inspection should document:

1. **Grade Reference Point**
   - Clearly identify the lowest adjacent grade
   - Photograph showing grade point relative to building
   - Mark location on site plan
   - Note any grade changes across property

2. **Lowest Floor Location**
   - Photograph showing relationship between grade and lowest floor
   - Identify building element that is "lowest floor" (top of floor/basement slab, bottom of door frame, etc.)
   - Measure distance from grade to lowest floor

3. **Measurement Documentation**
   - Method used (laser, tape, survey)
   - Number of measurement points
   - Reference points used
   - Vertical control (how verified)

4. **Final Site Plan**
   - Show grade point(s)
   - Show lowest floor location
   - Show elevation measurements
   - Note any significant site conditions (slope, fill, etc.)

### Using Your Documentation

After construction and inspector verification:

1. Provide copy of inspection documentation to property owner/surveyor
2. Surveyor completes EC Section D incorporating your measurements
3. EC and your inspection documentation together create complete as-built record
4. File both in your EC documentation system

## CRS Requirements: Maintaining EC Documentation

If your community participates in NFIP's Community Rating System (CRS), elevation certificate maintenance is a key requirement.

### CRS Activity 330: Elevation Certificates

The CRS awards points for communities that maintain ECs for:
- All new buildings in the SFHA
- All substantially improved buildings in the SFHA

### What You Must Maintain

- Original or certified copy of EC
- Building diagram and photographs (if available)
- Any correction documents or attached memos
- Documentation of your quality review
- Inspection documentation (as-built verification)

### File Organization

Develop system allowing easy retrieval by address/parcel number:

**Option 1: Physical Files by Flood Zone**
- File drawer/cabinet for SFHA ECs
- Organize by address or parcel number
- Cross-reference by property owner name

**Option 2: Digital Scans**
- Scan all ECs and attachments
- Organize in folder structure by address/parcel
- Searchable database linking parcel number to EC filename
- Backup copies (cloud or external drive)

**Option 3: Hybrid System**
- Scan all ECs to digital
- Maintain original documents in secure storage
- Database for fast retrieval
- Physical backup for originals

### CRS Documentation

For CRS credit, you must be able to demonstrate:
- Total number of new buildings in SFHA (from permits)
- Number of ECs obtained (from your file)
- Percentage of compliance
- Quality control process (brief description)
- Examples of ECs in your file showing complete documentation

## Equipment and Utilities Compliance Documentation

Machinery and Equipment (M&E) must be elevated above BFE in flood-prone areas.

### Your Role: Verify M&E Compliance

**During Construction Inspection:**
- Identify all mechanical systems (furnace, water heater, heat pump, A/C, elevator)
- Verify planned location and elevation
- If below BFE, verify flood-resistant design (sealed to prevent water entry) or other protection
- Document in field notes

**At Final Inspection:**
- Photograph equipment locations showing elevation above finished grade
- Verify against EC Section E documentation
- Note any equipment that appears below expected elevation
- Confirm adequate protection (if below BFE by necessity)

### EC Documentation

Section E should document:
- Each equipment type
- Elevation above grade (or datum)
- Location relative to building footprint
- Method of determination (measured, marked, estimated)

### Non-Compliance Issues

If equipment is below BFE without flood-resistant design:
- This is floodplain management violation
- Requires correction before occupancy permit
- May require structural modification or equipment relocation
- Document violation and required correction

## Variance Documentation

If your community issues variances allowing development below BFE, elevation certificates have special importance.

### Variance Requirements

An EC is typically required as condition of variance approval:

1. **Variance Application:** Demonstrates "exceptional hardship" or other variance criteria
2. **Conditional EC:** Proposed building elevation documented
3. **Variance Approval:** Community grants variance, EC requirement as condition
4. **As-Built EC:** After construction, final EC verifying compliance

### Your EC Review Role

**For Proposed Variance:**
- Verify EC shows proposed elevation
- Confirm elevation as close to BFE as feasible
- Note any additional requirements (flood-resistant construction, etc.)
- File copy with variance documentation

**For As-Built Variance:**
- Verify final EC matches variance approval conditions
- Confirm building constructed as approved
- Note any conditions imposed (flood openings, equipment elevation, etc.)
- Final approval only after confirmed compliance

### EC Storage

Maintain variance ECs with variance documentation:
- Link EC to specific variance number/date
- Cross-reference in your variance file
- Easy retrieval if variance circumstances questioned later

## Zone AO Special Responsibilities

Zone AO areas (shallow flood areas) have unique requirements:

### Zone AO Characteristics

- Shallow flooding (typically 1–3 feet depth)
- No designated BFE (flood depth specified instead)
- Usually floodplain without well-defined channel
- Different construction standards than A/AE zones

### Zone AO EC Requirements

For Zone AO:
- EC may or may not be required for insurance rating (check FEMA guidance)
- No survey typically required—owner can certify elevation
- But you should verify compliance through inspections

### Your Zone AO Responsibilities

**Permit Issuance:**
- Verify building will be elevated to BFE (or depth + height)
- Provide elevation requirements to applicant

**During Construction:**
- Inspect that lowest floor is elevated appropriately
- Verify in writing—document inspection

**After Construction:**
- Photograph showing elevation compliance
- Request verification or signed statement from owner
- File in your Zone AO records

**No Formal EC Required (Usually)**
But your inspection documentation creates the compliance record.

## Accessory Structures: When ECs Are Required

Accessory structures (garages, sheds, pools) may or may not require elevation certificates.

### When ECs Are Required

- Primary residential structure in SFHA
- Any substantially improved structure in SFHA
- Any enclosed structure below BFE
- Any structure with M&E below BFE

### When ECs Not Required

- Open structures (carports, gazebos, decks without enclosed area)
- Accessory structures not in floodplain
- Minor repairs to non-substantially-improved structures
- Fences, walls, landscaping

### Common Situation: Detached Garage in SFHA

**If Garage Will Be Elevated:**
- EC recommended to document elevation
- Simplifies insurance rating if garage has equipment or utilities
- Proves compliance with permit conditions

**If Garage Will Be At Grade:**
- Must meet Zone-specific requirements
- May require flood-resistant construction, flood openings, etc.
- EC not required but your inspection documenting compliance is

### Your Responsibility

At permit stage:
- Determine if accessory structure requires elevation
- Inform applicant of requirements
- Require EC or other compliance documentation as appropriate
- Verify at final inspection

## Community Communication and Professional Development

### Surveyor and Engineer Coordination

Develop relationships with local survey and engineering professionals:

1. **Provide Guidance Document**
   - Share your EC expectations and quality standards
   - Explain your review process
   - Provide examples of well-completed ECs
   - Clarify datum, diagram, and documentation requirements

2. **Host Training or Meeting**
   - Annual meeting with local professionals
   - Review common errors and how to avoid them
   - Discuss community expectations and map updates
   - Q&A on technical questions

3. **Share FEMA Resources**
   - Distribute FEMA Publication 467-1 (EC guidance)
   - Share technical bulletins on relevant topics
   - Reference 44 CFR Part 60 for regulatory context

4. **Provide Feedback**
   - When ECs are excellent, provide positive feedback
   - When corrections needed, explain clearly and constructively
   - Help professionals improve through constructive dialogue

### Community Outreach

Inform property owners about ECs:

1. **Permit Materials**
   - Include EC information in permit packets
   - Explain why EC may be required
   - Provide list of qualified professionals
   - Estimate cost and timeline

2. **Website Resources**
   - Link to FEMA EC resources
   - Provide list of local professionals
   - Example of completed EC (redacted for privacy)
   - Frequently asked questions

3. **Lender Notification**
   - Notify lenders when EC is required
   - Explain why (permit condition, insurance requirement, etc.)
   - Provide copies to support property transactions

## Common Floodplain Manager Questions

**Q: What if surveyor/engineer is not from my state?**

A: Check your state's laws. Some states allow out-of-state professionals for flood elevations; others require state licensure. Contact your state's professional licensing board. If out-of-state professional is used, require verification of their home state license and any reciprocity agreements.

**Q: Can I reject an EC because I disagree with the measurements?**

A: Only if you have evidence of error (previous survey, your inspection shows different elevation, mathematical error). If you believe EC is inaccurate, request re-measurement or provide your measurements for comparison. Do not reject based on judgment alone—if you believe there's an error, work with the professional to investigate.

**Q: How long should I keep ECs on file?**

A: Permanently. An EC may be needed decades later for property sale, reconstruction, or map change requests. Maintain indefinitely in organized system with backups.

**Q: What if property owner refuses to obtain EC?**

A: Cannot issue occupancy permit or final approval without EC (if required). EC requirement should be clear in permit conditions. Explain that EC is necessary for compliance and insurance. May need to escalate to code enforcement if building is constructed without it.

**Q: What if EC shows building below BFE?**

A: Verify the EC is accurate (compare to your inspections). If confirmed below BFE, determine if floodproofing, flood openings, or other protection is provided. May require modification or variance (if available). Do not approve occupancy until compliance is documented.

**Q: Can I request cheaper/faster ECs?**

A: You can encourage efficiency, but cannot dictate professional standards. ECs must be completed by licensed professionals meeting state standards. Accept ECs that meet professional standards regardless of cost/timeline. If professional quality is questionable, request verification.

## Summary: Your Mission

Your role is to ensure that Elevation Certificates in your community are:
- **Complete:** All required sections present and signed
- **Accurate:** Internally consistent and mathematically correct
- **Professional:** Completed by qualified, licensed professionals
- **Documented:** Filed and maintained for future reference
- **Compliant:** Meeting FEMA and 44 CFR Part 60 standards

When you do this well, you:
- Protect property owners from over-insurance
- Ensure accurate rating for insurers
- Demonstrate compliance for FEMA
- Support your community's floodplain management program
- Create a valuable historical record for future decisions

# Elevation Certificate Guide for Insurance Agents

Technical guidance for flood insurance agents on reading, interpreting, and using Elevation Certificates for rating, underwriting, and client management.

## Overview: The Agent's Role

As a flood insurance agent, you intersect with Elevation Certificates at multiple points:

1. **Rating Assessment:** Using EC data to determine accurate premiums under Risk Rating 2.0
2. **Underwriting:** Verifying building elevation and compliance with floodplain standards
3. **Client Recommendations:** Advising when ECs can lower premiums or improve coverage
4. **Documentation:** Maintaining ECs in policy files for compliance and future claims
5. **Communication:** Explaining EC significance to clients and lenders

This guide provides the technical foundation for all these responsibilities.

## How to Read an Elevation Certificate

### The Five Critical Sections

A complete, usable Elevation Certificate contains five essential sections:

**Section A: Building Location and Characteristics**
- Property street address, parcel number, and legal description
- Community and county
- Flood zone from current FIRM
- Base Flood Elevation (BFE) and vertical datum

*What you check:* Verify property address, flood zone, and BFE match your policy records and current FIRM. Flag mismatches before rating.

**Section B: Building Description**
- Building type (residential, commercial, etc.)
- Year constructed
- Number of stories
- Foundation type (slab, pier, pilings, crawlspace, basement)
- Building diagram showing footprint and elevation reference points

*What you check:* Confirm foundation type and diagram are appropriate for the flood zone. Elevations should be measured from defined grade reference points shown in the diagram.

**Section C: Vertical Reference Information**
- Vertical datum used (NAVD 1988 or NGVD 1929)
- Datum conversion factors if applicable
- Reference elevation (often based on GPS or benchmarks)

*What you check:* Ensure the datum matches your community's FIRM datum. If different, confirm conversion factor is documented. NAVD 1988 is standard on current FIRMs; older ECs in NGVD 1929 require conversion.

**Section D: Elevation Information and Certification**
- First Floor Height (FFH): Building's lowest floor elevation above adjacent grade
- Building elevation relative to BFE (how many feet above or below)
- Lowest floor elevation above vertical datum
- Basement elevation (if applicable)
- Surveyor/engineer signature, license number, and certification date

*What you check:* This is where most of the rating information lives. Verify:
  - FFH is clearly documented and reasonable
  - Building elevation relative to BFE is mathematically correct
  - Professional seal and signature are present
  - License number is valid for your state

**Section E: Machinery and Equipment Information**
- Elevation of furnace, water heater, heat pump, A/C, elevator (if present)
- Location (inside or outside building footprint)
- Documentation method (measured, estimated, source)

*What you check:* Confirm the lowest M&E elevation is documented (the lowest single piece of equipment drives the rating). Verify elevations are above BFE where required.

**Section F: Flood Opening Documentation**
- Location of permanent openings (vents, windows, doors, grates)
- Dimensions of openings
- Purpose (flood relief vents, crawlspace vents, etc.)
- Elevation above grade or BFE

*What you check:* For elevated buildings with enclosures, verify adequate venting (typically 1 sq. inch per sq. foot of enclosed area, per FEMA TB 1-93). Proper vents can affect rating.

**Section G: Surveyor/Engineer Comments and Certification**
- Additional notes and observations
- Any significant site conditions
- Scope of services provided
- Professional certification statement

*What you check:* Look for any caveats or limitations ("subject to," "unable to access," "estimated"). These may affect the EC's reliability.

### The Building Diagram

The diagram in Section B is critical. It should show:

- Building footprint and orientation
- Lowest floor location clearly marked
- Grade reference point(s) showing where measurements originate
- Direction of measurement (showing lowest point of adjacent grade)
- Any other reference points

Check that measurements in Sections D and E reference the points shown in the diagram. Inconsistent references suggest sloppy work.

## Key Fields That Affect Premiums

Under FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0, multiple EC fields contribute to the premium calculation:

### 1. First Floor Height (FFH) — Most Important

**Definition:** The elevation of the building's lowest floor above the lowest adjacent grade.

**Rating Impact:** Directly drives premium under Risk Rating 2.0. Each foot of FFH above BFE typically reduces premium 10–20%.

**What to Verify:**
- FFH is clearly documented in Section D
- Grade reference point is defined
- Measurement uses standard methodology (tape measure, laser, survey)
- Height is measured from lowest adjacent grade (not average grade)

**Example Scenario:**
- BFE: 10 feet
- FFH: 15 feet (5 feet above BFE)
- Premium: ~$600/year for $250,000 residential building

If you later discover FFH was actually 10 feet (at BFE):
- Premium should be: ~$1,800/year
- Exposure: $1,200/year underage

### 2. Flood Zone

**Definition:** The hazard designation from the FIRM.

**Rating Impact:** Some zones (V, AO, high-velocity areas) have higher base rates.

**What to Verify:**
- EC lists the same flood zone as your policy record
- Zone matches current FIRM (check FEMA's Flood Map Service Center if unsure)
- If zone changed since EC was issued, note it

### 3. Building Elevation Relative to BFE

**Definition:** The vertical distance between the building's lowest floor and the Base Flood Elevation.

**Rating Impact:** Directly affects premium tier. Positive elevations (above BFE) receive lower rates.

**What to Verify:**
- Mathematically correct (FFH + lowest adjacent grade elevation – BFE)
- Consistent across all references in EC
- Matches the datum used on your community's FIRM

### 4. Lowest Floor Type

**Definition:** Whether the lowest floor is a basement, crawlspace, enclosure, or main floor.

**Rating Impact:** Basements receive different (generally higher) rates; enclosures receive different treatment under NFIP rules.

**What to Verify:**
- Building diagram clearly shows lowest floor type
- Description in Section B matches diagram
- If basement, is it fully enclosed below BFE?
- If enclosure, are flood vents documented in Section F?

### 5. Machinery and Equipment (M&E) Elevation

**Definition:** The elevation of the lowest piece of essential equipment.

**Rating Impact:** Under Risk Rating 2.0, equipment elevation affects damage-potential modeling.

**What to Verify:**
- All critical equipment is documented: furnace, water heater, heat pump, A/C, elevator
- The lowest single item elevation is identified
- Location (inside or outside building footprint) is noted
- Elevation is above BFE where required

### 6. Flood Openings

**Definition:** Permanent vents or openings below BFE allowing flood water equalization.

**Rating Impact:** Properly-sized openings reduce structural damage risk and may affect rating.

**What to Verify:**
- For enclosures, confirm openings meet FEMA standards (TB 1-93)
- Quantity and dimensions are adequate (1 sq. inch per sq. foot minimum)
- Openings are actually present and not blocked
- Elevation and location are documented

## First Floor Height Under Risk Rating 2.0

Risk Rating 2.0 is FEMA's property-specific rating methodology. FFH is central to how it works.

### How FFH Affects Risk Rating 2.0

The system models:
- **Probability of inundation:** How likely the property will flood given rainfall, proximity to water bodies, and elevation
- **Depth of inundation:** How deep water will be if a flood occurs
- **Damage potential:** How much damage will result from that depth of inundation

FFH directly influences damage potential. A building elevated 4 feet above BFE experiences much less damage in a flood than one at ground level.

### Accurate FFH Is Critical

Errors in FFH are among the most common rating mistakes:

**Understated FFH (too low):**
- Results in higher premiums than warranted
- Client believes rates are unfair
- Competitive disadvantage for your agency

**Overstated FFH (too high):**
- Results in lower premiums than warranted
- Creates underwriting exposure
- Violates NFIP regulations
- Causes claim denials if inundation depth exceeds expectations

### Verifying FFH Accuracy

When you receive an EC:

1. **Check the Documentation Source**
   - Is FFH based on actual survey/measurement?
   - Or is it estimated or self-certified?
   - Self-certified FFH (Section H) is valid but may have lower precision

2. **Verify Reasonableness**
   - Does FFH match building appearance?
   - Are elevated homes actually elevated?
   - Are older homes likely to be at grade level?
   - Is FFH consistent with the diagram?

3. **Check the Math**
   - Is the relationship between FFH and building elevation correct?
   - Does the BFE conversion (if datum differs) make sense?

4. **Cross-Reference**
   - Do Section D, Section E, and the building diagram all tell the same story?
   - Are any elevations inconsistent?

**Red Flags:**
- FFH documented but no supporting measurements or methodology
- FFH that seems inconsistent with building type or era
- Diagram that doesn't clearly show where measurements originated
- No professional license or seal
- Datum conversion not documented or incorrect

## When to Recommend Clients Get an Elevation Certificate

### Situations Requiring a New EC

1. **Building Elevation Is Unknown or Questionable**
   - Older properties without documentation
   - Recent construction without as-built EC
   - Buildings that have been modified or improved
   - *Recommendation:* Obtain EC before rating; don't estimate

2. **Client Suspects They're Overpaying**
   - Building appears elevated
   - Neighbor has lower rates
   - Previous owner mentioned elevation
   - *Recommendation:* "An EC might reveal significant savings—the investment often pays for itself in the first year"

3. **Client Is Purchasing a Property**
   - No EC is on file
   - Existing EC is more than 10 years old
   - Map has changed since existing EC
   - *Recommendation:* Require EC as condition of underwriting; lender may require it anyway

4. **Property Has Been Substantially Improved**
   - Elevated above original BFE
   - New foundation or pilings
   - Rebuilt after flood or substantial damage
   - *Recommendation:* New EC will document improvements and likely lower rates

5. **Community's FIRM Has Been Updated**
   - BFE has changed significantly
   - Property has moved to different flood zone
   - Existing EC's BFE no longer matches current map
   - *Recommendation:* New EC ensures accurate rating under current FIRM

6. **Seeking a Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA)**
   - Client wants to be removed from flood zone
   - Building is likely above BFE
   - *Recommendation:* EC is required evidence for LOMA application

### Client Communication

When recommending an EC:

> "I'd like to get an Elevation Certificate on your property. This is a professional measurement that documents how high your building is above the flood level. It often reveals savings—if your home is elevated, your premiums could drop significantly. It typically costs $500–$2,000 and takes a few weeks. Would you like me to help you find a surveyor?"

**Key Points:**
- Lead with potential savings
- Explain the process clearly
- Offer to help find a professional
- Emphasize it's worth the investment

## Section H: The New Self-Certification Option

FEMA now allows Section H (Self-Certification of First Floor Height) for certain properties.

### What Section H Is

A streamlined option allowing property owners to self-report First Floor Height (FFH) without a full professional survey. The form is completed with basic height measurements.

### When Section H Can Be Used

- Simple residential buildings (single-family homes, townhomes)
- Buildings with straightforward, measurable lowest floor
- No complex site conditions
- Property owners willing to provide accurate measurements

### Section H Advantages for Agents

- **Faster:** Days instead of weeks
- **Cheaper:** $200–$500 vs. $500–$2,000
- **Client satisfaction:** Quicker rate determination
- **Still valid:** FEMA-approved method for FFH documentation

### Section H Limitations

- **FFH only:** Does not document equipment elevations, flood openings, or other details
- **Lower precision:** Measurements may be less precise than professional survey
- **Incomplete documentation:** No site plan or photographs
- **Limited use:** Insufficient for some underwriting needs or claim situations

### When to Recommend Full EC Instead of Section H

- Property has equipment requiring elevation documentation
- Building has flood openings requiring specific documentation
- Client is making substantive property claims
- Client is seeking LOMA (requires full professional EC)
- Lender or local authority requires full EC
- Building has complex construction or site conditions
- Property will be resold in foreseeable future

## Common Issues Agents Encounter With Elevation Certificates

### Issue 1: Datum Mismatch

**Problem:** EC uses NGVD 1929 datum; your FIRM uses NAVD 1988. The elevations don't line up.

**Solution:**
- Confirm the datum conversion factor documented in Section C
- Apply conversion: NAVD 1988 elevation ≈ NGVD 1929 elevation + conversion factor (typically 1–3 feet depending on location)
- Contact FEMA's Flood Map Service Center if unsure about conversion
- Request new EC from professional if conversion is unclear

### Issue 2: Missing or Incomplete Sections

**Problem:** EC is missing professional certification, equipment documentation, or signatures.

**Solution:**
- Return to professional for completion
- Do not use incomplete EC for rating or underwriting
- Request clarification of any missing data

### Issue 3: Unclear Building Diagram

**Problem:** Diagram doesn't clearly show where measurements originated or how grades were measured.

**Solution:**
- Request clarification from surveyor/engineer
- Ask for additional photographs showing measurement methodology
- If unclear, consider obtaining new EC from different professional

### Issue 4: Equipment Not Documented

**Problem:** EC doesn't list furnace, water heater, or other equipment elevations.

**Solution:**
- Ask surveyor if equipment was not present or was simply not documented
- Request supplemental documentation if equipment exists but was omitted
- If equipment elevation is critical to rating, request new or supplemented EC

### Issue 5: Inconsistent Elevations

**Problem:** Section D says FFH is 12 feet, but Section E says equipment is at 14 feet.

**Solution:**
- Identify the inconsistency to the surveyor/engineer
- Request clarification or correction
- Do not rate based on inconsistent data

### Issue 6: EC References Old Flood Map Data

**Problem:** EC documents an old BFE that has since changed due to map update.

**Solution:**
- Confirm current BFE with FEMA's Flood Map Service Center
- Recalculate building elevation relative to new BFE
- Existing EC is still valid; just reference it against current BFE
- Consider whether client should seek LOMA if still elevated above new BFE

### Issue 7: Professional License Cannot Be Verified

**Problem:** Surveyor or engineer's license number is invalid or cannot be found.

**Solution:**
- Request copy of license from professional
- Verify through state licensing board
- If unverifiable, do not use EC
- Request new EC from licensed professional in your state

## How Map Changes Affect Existing Elevation Certificates

### When FEMA Updates a Flood Map

Communities periodically receive revised FIRMs based on new hydrologic data, development, or structural changes. When this happens:

1. **The EC itself remains valid**
   - The document is still accurate as of the measurement date
   - The building elevation hasn't changed

2. **But the relevance changes**
   - Old BFE may no longer apply
   - Property may move to different flood zone
   - New building elevation relative to new BFE must be calculated

### How to Handle Map Updates

**Scenario 1: New BFE Is Higher**
- Calculate new elevation relative to new BFE
- May result in higher rates if elevation to new BFE is less
- Check if property might now be outside SFHA (possible LOMA opportunity)

**Scenario 2: New BFE Is Lower**
- Calculate new elevation relative to new BFE
- Should result in lower rates
- Good opportunity to re-rate and improve client relationships

**Scenario 3: Property Moves Out of SFHA**
- Best case for client
- EC can be used to apply for LOMA
- Property may be removed from NFIP requirement

**Scenario 4: Zone Changes (e.g., AE to X)**
- Significant rating implications
- Check current FIRM and map update guidance
- Update client's policy to reflect new zone

### When to Request Updated EC

After a map update, request a new EC if:
- More than 5 years old and significant changes occurred
- Building has been modified since original EC
- Current EC doesn't address new BFE or zone
- Change is material enough to affect rating or client perception

Otherwise, existing EC can be used with BFE adjustments.

## Summary: The Agent's Responsibility

Your role is to:

1. **Understand EC contents** and what they mean for rating
2. **Verify EC accuracy** before using for underwriting
3. **Advise clients** on when ECs provide value
4. **Maintain EC documentation** in policy files
5. **Communicate professionally** about elevations and rates
6. **Comply with regulations** using accurate EC data for rating

Elevation Certificates are among the most important documents in flood insurance. When you manage them well, you provide tremendous value to clients while protecting your agency from rating errors and claims issues.

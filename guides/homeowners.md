# Elevation Certificate Guide for Homeowners

A plain-language guide to understanding, obtaining, and using Elevation Certificates for flood insurance and property compliance.

## What Is an Elevation Certificate?

An Elevation Certificate is an official FEMA form that documents the elevation of your building's lowest floor compared to the Base Flood Elevation (BFE) in your area. Think of it as a "height measurement" that determines how much flood risk your property faces and how much flood insurance will cost.

The form is completed by a licensed surveyor or engineer and includes:
- Your building's location and flood zone
- The elevation of your home's lowest floor
- Location and elevation of utilities (furnace, water heater, air conditioner, etc.)
- Flood opening information (vents or windows that allow water to drain)
- A diagram of your building showing these measurements

## Why You Might Need an Elevation Certificate

**Lower Insurance Premiums**

This is the biggest reason most homeowners get an EC. If your building is elevated above the Base Flood Elevation, your flood insurance premium drops dramatically.

Example annual flood insurance premiums for a house with $250,000 in coverage:
- 8 feet below BFE: $9,500/year
- 2 feet above BFE: $1,410/year
- 4 feet above BFE: $427/year

A single Elevation Certificate ($500–$2,000) can pay for itself in savings within the first year.

**Property Compliance**

If you live in a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA), your mortgage lender likely required flood insurance. An EC proves you're complying with this requirement and may be needed for:
- Your lender or insurance company to update your property record
- Securing a mortgage modification or refinance
- Showing compliance if there are questions about your building

**Selling Your Property**

Buyers and their lenders want proof that the property is properly elevated or floodproofed. An EC provides that documentation and can make your property more attractive to buyers by showing lower risk and insurance costs.

**Post-Disaster Rebuilding**

After a flood, FEMA and your insurance company will ask about the elevation of your rebuilt structure. An EC on file beforehand speeds up claims and shows that rebuilding was done correctly.

**Building Code Compliance**

Many communities require ECs during construction to verify that new buildings or substantially improved buildings meet floodplain management standards.

## How to Check If You Already Have One

Before paying for a new Elevation Certificate, check whether one already exists:

**Contact Your Local Floodplain Manager**
- Call your city or county building/floodplain management office
- Ask: "Do you have an Elevation Certificate on file for my property?"
- They may have one from a previous owner or when the house was built

**Ask the Previous Owner**
- If you recently purchased, contact the previous owner or their real estate agent
- They may have a copy from when they had flood insurance

**Check with Your Builder or Developer**
- If you're in a newer subdivision, the builder may have ECs for all homes in the development

**Review Your Insurance Documents**
- Your flood insurance policy or recent rate determination may reference an existing EC

If an EC exists, you can often get a copy from the floodplain manager, your insurance agent, or by contacting the professional who completed it.

## The Elevation Certificate Process

### Step 1: Initial Contact and Information Gathering

Contact a surveyor or engineer and provide:
- Your property address
- Current FIRM panel showing your property
- Information about your building (age, construction type, number of stories)
- Any available site plans or building drawings

The professional will review your property's flood zone and advise whether an EC is appropriate for your property.

### Step 2: Site Visit and Field Measurements

The surveyor or engineer will:
- Visit your property
- Measure the elevation of your building's lowest floor above ground level
- Document utility and equipment locations
- Identify and measure any flood openings or vents
- Take photographs
- Note site conditions (slope, grades, etc.)

This visit typically takes 1–2 hours. Provide access to:
- Basement or crawlspace (if present)
- Attic or upper floors for reference measurements
- Utility areas (furnace room, etc.)

### Step 3: Elevation Determination and Documentation

The professional will:
- Compare measurements to your community's Base Flood Elevation
- Document the relationship (how many feet above or below BFE)
- Prepare a site plan showing the measurements and building location
- Compile photographs and supporting documentation

### Step 4: Form Completion and Certification

The professional completes the FEMA Elevation Certificate form, including:
- **Section A:** Property and community information
- **Section B:** Flood zone and BFE information
- **Section C:** Vertical datum and elevation references
- **Section D:** Building elevation measurements and certification
- **Section E:** Mechanical and electrical equipment elevations
- **Section F:** Flood opening documentation
- **Section G:** Surveyor/engineer comments and certification

The form is signed and sealed by the licensed professional.

### Step 5: Receipt and Submission

You receive:
- Official signed and sealed EC form (original or certified copy)
- Site plan and photographs
- Copies for your records
- Instructions for submission

You should:
- Keep a copy for your records
- Provide a copy to your flood insurance agent
- Submit a copy to your local floodplain manager (may be required)
- Store in a safe place (important for future claims or sales)

## The New Self-Certification Option (Section H)

FEMA has introduced a new option that may save you time and money.

**What It Is**

Section H of the Elevation Certificate allows property owners to provide First Floor Height (FFH) information directly, without requiring a full survey. This is only the height measurement—not a complete Elevation Certificate.

**Who Can Use It**

- Simple residential buildings (single-family homes, townhomes)
- Buildings with straightforward elevation
- Properties where the owner can provide reliable height measurements

**What You Need to Do**

1. Measure your building's lowest floor elevation using standard methods
   - Measure from grade level up to the bottom of the first floor
   - Use a laser level or hire a minimal survey for accurate measurement
   - Document the measurement method

2. Complete Section H with your measurements

3. Have a licensed professional review and certify your measurements

**Advantages**

- Faster than a full survey (days instead of weeks)
- Lower cost ($200–$500 vs. $500–$2,000)
- Simpler process for straightforward properties

**When You Still Need a Full EC**

Section H alone is not sufficient if you:
- Have a complex building or multiple structures
- Have specific flood opening requirements
- Need equipment (furnace, water heater) elevations documented
- Are making insurance claims
- Are substantially improving or reconstructing your home
- Have a lender or local requirement for a full EC

Check with your insurance agent or floodplain manager about which option works for your property.

## How the Elevation Certificate Affects Your Flood Insurance Premium

FEMA's current rating system (Risk Rating 2.0) uses your building's First Floor Height as a key factor in determining your premium.

### The Basic Rule

**The higher your building above the Base Flood Elevation, the lower your premium.**

### Premium Examples

These examples show annual premiums for a $250,000 residential building in a SFHA:

| Building Elevation | Annual Premium |
|-------------------|----------------|
| 8 feet below BFE | $9,500 |
| 4 feet below BFE | $3,200 |
| At or 1 foot below BFE | $1,800 |
| 1–2 feet above BFE | $1,410 |
| 3–4 feet above BFE | $820 |
| 5+ feet above BFE | $427 |

**Cost-Benefit Example:**

If your EC shows your building is 4 feet above BFE instead of 1 foot below:
- Difference in annual premium: $2,383
- EC cost: $1,500
- Payback period: Less than 9 months
- 10-year savings: $20,330

### Factors Beyond Elevation

Risk Rating 2.0 also considers:
- Distance to water bodies and flood sources
- Building construction type
- Flood opening presence and size
- Equipment and utility elevation
- Mitigation features (raised utilities, floodproofing)

Your EC documents many of these factors, allowing accurate rating.

### Substantial Improvement Discounts

If you substantially improve (50%+ of building value) or elevate your structure:
- You may qualify for Community Rating System (CRS) discounts
- Discounts can reach 30–45% of premium
- Your local floodplain manager can advise on your community's CRS rating
- An EC documenting the improvement qualifies you for the discount

## Post-Disaster Considerations

### Before a Flood

- Have an EC on file
- Keep a copy in a waterproof container
- Provide a copy to your insurance agent
- Document your property's condition with photos

### After a Flood

An existing EC will help:
- **Speed insurance claims:** Proves pre-flood building elevation
- **Guide rebuilding:** Shows required elevation standards for your community
- **Obtain permits:** Simplifies the rebuilding permit process
- **Secure loans:** Disaster recovery assistance may require elevation verification

If no EC exists before a flood:
- You'll likely be required to complete one before rebuilding
- Insurance claims may be delayed without elevation documentation
- Rebuilding permits will require new elevation verification

## Building Higher Than BFE as a Long-Term Investment

### Why Consider Extra Elevation?

Building above the minimum BFE requirement provides:

1. **Lower Insurance Costs**
   - Each foot of additional elevation can reduce premiums 10–20%
   - Savings compound over decades
   - May offset the cost of extra elevation in 5–10 years

2. **Better Long-Term Risk Protection**
   - Provides buffer if flood maps are updated (BFE increases)
   - Protects against climate change and increased precipitation
   - Reduces stress and uncertainty

3. **Faster Resale**
   - Properties well-above BFE appeal to buyers
   - Easier to demonstrate low risk
   - Reduced need for flood insurance negotiation

4. **Potential Eligibility for Future Hazard Mitigation Grants**
   - Some disaster recovery programs provide grants for elevation
   - Having EC documentation in advance speeds the process

### Practical Considerations

- Extra elevation requires a higher foundation or piers
- Adds cost during construction ($5,000–$25,000+ depending on design)
- May require ramp or stairs for building access
- Consider long-term value vs. upfront cost

Discuss with your builder, architect, or engineer about cost-effective elevation design.

## Common Questions

**Q: Can I do my own measurements for the Elevation Certificate?**

A: Not for a valid, official EC. The form must be completed and certified by a licensed surveyor or engineer. Your state may allow self-certification (Section H) for First Floor Height only, but a full EC requires professional certification.

**Q: How often do I need a new Elevation Certificate?**

A: If nothing has changed, one EC is valid permanently. However, you may need a new one if:
- You substantially improve or rebuild the building
- Your local floodplain manager requires updated information
- You're applying for a flood map change (LOMA)
- The FIRM is updated and your BFE changes significantly

**Q: What if my building is in an A Zone without a detailed BFE?**

A: The EC is still valuable. It documents your building's elevation and can be used if detailed BFE is later available. Ask your floodplain manager or insurance agent about Zone AO procedures.

**Q: Will an Elevation Certificate help me get out of the flood zone?**

A: It may. If your EC shows your lowest floor above the BFE, you can apply for a Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA), which removes the property from the flood zone. This is a FEMA process separate from the EC but uses the EC as evidence.

**Q: Does an Elevation Certificate guarantee lower insurance rates?**

A: Yes. If the EC shows your building above the BFE, your insurance company must use that elevation for rating under Risk Rating 2.0. However, other factors (proximity to water, construction type, etc.) may still affect your rate.

**Q: Can I use an older Elevation Certificate?**

A: Older ECs are valid if nothing has changed and the form is complete. However, if datum conversion is unclear or the BFE has changed, a new EC may be recommended. Consult your insurance agent or floodplain manager.

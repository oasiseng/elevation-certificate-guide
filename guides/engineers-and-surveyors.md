# Elevation Certificate Guide for Engineers and Surveyors

Technical guidance for licensed professionals completing FEMA Elevation Certificates, including scope of practice, field procedures, documentation standards, and professional liability considerations.

## Scope of Practice and Professional Authorization

### Who Can Complete Elevation Certificates?

**Surveyors:**
- Licensed Professional Surveyor (PLS) or equivalent
- Authorized under state surveying laws
- Specialty: Horizontal and vertical positioning, boundary surveys, elevation measurements
- Must maintain Professional Surveyor license in state where work performed

**Engineers:**
- Licensed Professional Engineer (PE) in applicable discipline (civil, structural, etc.)
- Authorized if state law permits for EC completion
- Specialty: Structural analysis, building systems, site engineering
- Must maintain PE license in state where work performed

**Both professions can complete ECs if authorized by state law.** Check your state's surveying board and engineering board requirements before undertaking EC work.

### State-Specific Requirements

Requirements vary significantly by state. Before completing any EC:

1. **Contact Your State's Professional Board**
   - Surveying Board: Verify who can complete elevation certificates
   - Engineering Board: Verify if PE license required/sufficient
   - Some states: Surveyors only
   - Some states: Either profession acceptable
   - Some states: Specific discipline required (e.g., surveying PE)

2. **Review State Law and Regulations**
   - Look for EC-specific requirements
   - Review scope of practice for your profession
   - Check for required certifications or endorsements
   - Verify seal and signature requirements

3. **Professional Association Standards**
   - National Society of Professional Surveyors (NSPS)
   - American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE)
   - State professional associations
   - Review best practices and technical guidance

4. **FEMA Guidance**
   - FEMA Publication 467-1: EC guidance
   - Technical bulletins on specific topics (TB 1-93, TB 11-01)
   - Consistent with state law but not controlling

### Scope of Services to Document

In your EC certification (Section D), clearly state the scope of your services:

Examples of proper scope statements:
- "Professional surveyed building elevations using [equipment/method], measured from [datum] tied to [vertical control]. Vertical accuracy approximately [±X feet]."
- "Reviewed available construction documents and inspected building conditions. Lowest floor elevation determined from [methodology]. Subject to [limitations]."
- "Coordinated with contractor and reviewed as-built observations. Lowest floor elevation verified against [reference point]. See Section G for conditions."

Scope should specify:
- What was actually measured vs. estimated
- Equipment and methods used
- Vertical datum and control used
- Accuracy achievable
- Any limitations or assumptions

This tells the reader (insurance company, floodplain manager) exactly what work was performed and how reliable the data is.

## Field Procedures Overview

### Pre-Field Preparation

**1. Gather Available Information**
- FIRM showing property location, flood zone, BFE
- Site plan or subdivision plat (if available)
- Building plans or as-built drawings
- Existing elevation certificates (if property previously surveyed)
- Community floodplain management information
- Vertical datum used on FIRM (NAVD 1988 or NGVD 1929)

**2. Understand Community Requirements**
- Contact local floodplain manager or building official
- Understand specific requirements for that community
- Ask about datum conversions needed
- Clarify what documentation is required
- Ask about common errors or issues they've seen

**3. Plan Field Work**
- Identify site access points
- Plan equipment and crew needed
- Identify potential vertical control points (benchmarks, monuments)
- Estimate time needed for property complexity
- Plan photography locations

**4. Prepare Equipment**
- Verify all instruments are calibrated
- Have datum conversion tools/software available
- Prepare data sheets or field notes template
- Prepare camera for documentation photography
- Have backup equipment in case of failure

### Establishing Vertical Control

**Define Your Datum**
Before beginning measurements, establish which vertical datum you will use:
- Modern work typically uses NAVD 1988 (current FEMA standard)
- Older work may use NGVD 1929 (documented on older FIRMs)
- **Clearly document in Section C which datum you are using**

**Tie to Reliable Control**
Your measurements must originate from a known vertical reference:

**Option 1: GPS with Real-Time Kinematic (RTK)**
- Provides accurate vertical positioning (±0.1 feet or better)
- Must use appropriate vertical datum
- Document reference frame and processing methodology
- Suitable for most elevation work
- Highest accuracy and modern standard

**Option 2: Established Benchmark**
- Use USGS benchmark (if available in area)
- Verify benchmark stability and current elevation
- Tie multiple shots to same benchmark for verification
- Document benchmark number and elevation source

**Option 3: Differential Leveling**
- Run level from known benchmark to property
- Use differential or dumpy level instrument
- Establish intermediate reference points on property
- Provides good accuracy (±0.1 feet)
- More time-consuming than RTK

**Option 4: Transit/Theodolite with Distance**
- Less precise for vertical (±0.5+ feet)
- Acceptable for less critical work
- Should not be sole vertical control method
- Cross-check with other reference if possible

### Identifying Lowest Adjacent Grade

This is critical and often done poorly. **Lowest adjacent grade** means the lowest point of ground elevation immediately adjacent to the building footprint.

**Method 1: Visual Inspection with Measurement**
1. Walk around entire building perimeter
2. Identify visually where grade appears lowest
3. Measure elevation at that point
4. Repeat at multiple locations (minimum 4 points for simple building)
5. Use the lowest measured elevation as LAG

**Method 2: Systematic Grid**
1. Divide building perimeter into sections
2. Measure elevation at consistent intervals (every 10–20 feet)
3. Record all measurements
4. Identify lowest single point
5. Use for LAG

**Method 3: Contour Mapping**
1. Take elevation measurements at many points (20+ for typical building)
2. Create contour map showing grade elevation across property
3. Identify lowest point on contour
4. Provides best overall picture of site drainage

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Measuring only at building corners (may miss lower grade elsewhere)
- Using average grade instead of lowest point (incorrect)
- Measuring in drainage ditch or low spot not adjacent to building (incorrect)
- Not considering future grading or fill (document what currently exists)
- Failing to check all sides of building

**Document Your Process:**
In Section G, describe which method you used:
- "Measured elevation at eight points around building perimeter; recorded lowest point as LAG"
- "Visual inspection identified grade slope on east side; measured multiple points; lowest elevation used"
- "Systematic 20-foot grid established around building; lowest point identified and documented"

### Measuring First Floor Height (FFH)

**Definition:** The vertical distance from the lowest adjacent grade to the building's lowest floor.

**What Is "Lowest Floor"?**
- Top surface of lowest floor (or lowest floor slab)
- NOT the ground beneath the building
- Includes basements, crawlspaces, enclosures
- The floor upon which people stand or goods rest

**Measurement Method:**

1. **Establish grade reference point**
   - Mark on site plan and photograph
   - Record its elevation (tied to vertical datum)

2. **Identify lowest floor location**
   - Point on building directly above grade reference
   - Or directly above lowest adjacent grade point
   - Must be the lowest floor point if building varies

3. **Measure vertical distance**
   - From grade surface up to bottom of first floor
   - May measure from bottom of slab for basement
   - From top of floor for raised buildings
   - Use laser level, transit and rod, or tape measure (depending on equipment and precision needed)

4. **Calculate FFH**
   - FFH = (Lowest floor elevation datum) – (Lowest adjacent grade elevation datum)
   - Both elevations in same datum
   - Difference is FFH (in feet)

**Example Measurement Sequence:**

Building with crawlspace:
- Establish grade reference point: Grade elevation = 118.4' NAVD 1988
- Measure to bottom of floor joist (floor support): 126.2' NAVD 1988
- FFH = 126.2 – 118.4 = 7.8 feet

Building with basement:
- Establish grade reference point: Grade elevation = 115.6' NAVD 1988
- Measure to top of basement floor slab (lowest floor): 112.8' NAVD 1988
- FFH = 112.8 – 115.6 = –2.8 feet (basement floor is 2.8 feet below grade)

### Building Elevation Relative to BFE

Once you know the building elevation and BFE, calculate the relationship:

**Formula:**
Building elevation vs. BFE = Building lowest floor elevation – BFE elevation

**Example:**
- Building lowest floor: 125.3' NAVD 1988
- BFE from FIRM: 120.0' NAVD 1988
- Building is 5.3 feet ABOVE BFE ✓

**Example (Below BFE):**
- Building lowest floor: 118.2' NAVD 1988
- BFE from FIRM: 120.0' NAVD 1988
- Building is 1.8 feet BELOW BFE

**Important:** This calculation is straightforward BUT both elevations must use the same vertical datum. If they don't, you must convert first.

## Datum Matching and Conversion

This is where many errors occur. **Section C must document your vertical datum and confirm it matches the FIRM.**

### Checking FIRM Datum

**Modern FIRMs (typically after 2010):**
- Use NAVD 1988 datum
- Clearly stated on map
- Elevations labeled "NAVD 88" or similar

**Older FIRMs (1980s–2000s):**
- Often use NGVD 1929 datum
- May be unclear which datum
- Contact community or FEMA if uncertain

**How to Verify:**
1. Look at FIRM title block and legend
2. Check Flood Insurance Study (FIS) accompanying FIRM
3. Contact community floodplain manager
4. Contact FEMA's Flood Map Service Center: (877) 336-2627

### If Datum Mismatch Occurs

**Scenario:** Your survey used NAVD 1988 (standard), but community's FIRM is NGVD 1929 (older).

**Solution:**
1. Document BOTH datums in your EC
2. Provide datum conversion factor in Section C
3. Show calculations converting between datums
4. Typically NAVD 1988 ≈ NGVD 1929 + conversion factor (varies by location, typically +1 to +3 feet)

**Example:**
- Your measurement: 125.5' NAVD 1988
- Community FIRM datum: NGVD 1929
- Conversion factor for this location: +2.1' (from NOAA or USGS)
- Equivalent elevation in NGVD 1929: 125.5 – 2.1 = 123.4' NGVD 1929
- Document: "NAVD 1988 elevation converted to NGVD 1929 using NOAA factor +2.1 feet"

**Where to Find Conversion Factors:**
- NOAA (National Oceanic & Atmospheric Admin) provides state-by-county factors
- USGS Topo maps often show conversions
- FEMA's Map Service Center can provide factors
- Some surveying software includes conversion tables

### Best Practice: Document Everything

In your EC Section C, state clearly:

Example 1: No conversion needed
- "All elevations in NAVD 1988, matching community FIRM datum"
- "Vertical control established via RTK GPS tied to NAD 83 / NAVD 1988"

Example 2: Conversion performed
- "Measured elevations in NAVD 1988; converted to community FIRM datum (NGVD 1929) using NOAA conversion factor +2.1 feet"
- "All reported elevations are in NGVD 1929 matching FIRM"
- "Conversion factor source: NOAA Vertical Datum Transformation Tool"

## Crawlspace Measurement Techniques

Crawlspaces present special measurement challenges due to limited access.

### The Measurement Challenge

**Why difficult:**
- Limited headroom restricts equipment use
- Difficult to establish level reference
- Floor joists may vary in elevation
- Moisture and soil conditions
- Safety concerns

**What must be documented:**
- Elevation of lowest floor (top of rim joist or floor slab, whichever is lowest)
- Crawlspace vent locations
- Support pier elevations
- Grade elevation directly beneath crawlspace

### Method 1: Direct Measurement (If Access Available)

**Equipment:**
- Laser level or transit with rod
- Tape measure for fine measurements
- Flashlight for visibility
- Reference measuring stick

**Procedure:**
1. Access crawlspace at lowest point
2. Establish inside floor elevation (top of rim joist or floor slab)
3. Establish elevation of lowest adjacent grade above
4. Measure vertical distance between them
5. Repeat measurements at multiple locations if floor height varies

**Safety:**
- Verify crawlspace is safe to enter (no toxic gases, structural issues, hazards)
- Use headlamp and ensure adequate lighting
- If crawlspace is unsafe, decline to enter and note limitation

### Method 2: Measurement Through Openings (Limited Access)

**When direct access impossible:**
- Measure through crawlspace vents or access points
- Use laser level sighting through opening
- Measure vertical distance from grade up to floor joist level
- Requires careful sighting and documentation

**Limitations:**
- Less precise than direct measurement
- Document limitation in Section G
- Recommend access improvement or as-built verification

### Method 3: Exterior Grade and Building Observation

**When crawlspace cannot be accessed:**
1. Measure grade elevation around building perimeter
2. Measure distance from grade to underside of building
3. Identify support type (piers, pilings, continuous wall)
4. Estimate rim joist elevation based on visual observation
5. Document as estimated with limitations noted

**Limitations:**
- Less reliable than direct measurement
- Document methodology and assumptions
- Note in Section G: "Crawlspace not accessed; floor elevation estimated based on exterior observations and pier spacing"
- Flag for community inspection verification

### Crawlspace Diagram

In your building diagram (Section B), show:
- Crawlspace with its overall dimensions
- Access point(s) used
- Grade elevation points around perimeter
- Floor joist or rim joist elevation
- Support system (piers, pilings, spacing)
- Any unusual site conditions

### Documentation

Document your crawlspace work:
- "Accessed via [type of opening]. Measured crawlspace floor height at [number] points. Lowest elevation recorded as [value]. Grade measured at [points]."
- Or: "Crawlspace access limited to [vents/opening]. Elevation estimated based on [methodology]. Recommend verification by community inspector."

## Flood Opening Documentation

Flood openings (vents, windows, doors) below BFE allow water to equalize pressure during floods, reducing structural damage.

### What Constitutes a Flood Opening

**Acceptable openings:**
- Permanent louvered vents
- Grates or screen vents
- Fixed open frames
- Doors or windows designed to break away
- Any opening meeting FEMA Technical Bulletin 1-93 standards

**Size requirements (per TB 1-93):**
- Minimum 1 square inch per square foot of enclosed area
- Minimum individual opening 3" × 3" (or equivalent)
- Located on all sides of building if possible
- Bottom edge within 1 foot of grade
- Cannot be blocked by screens or bushes

### Measuring Flood Openings

**For each opening, document:**

1. **Location**
   - Building side (north, south, east, west)
   - Distance from corner (e.g., "15 feet east of northeast corner")
   - Photograph showing location

2. **Dimensions**
   - Width and height (or diameter if round)
   - Calculate square inches (W × H = area in square inches)
   - If grate or louvered: measure clear opening (not overall frame)

3. **Elevation**
   - Height of bottom edge above grade
   - For vents in rim joist: measure bottom of vent elevation
   - Document relative to BFE (below, at, or above)

4. **Type**
   - Louvered vent, grate, door, window, etc.
   - Fixed or designed to break away?
   - Accessible/blockable or permanent?

### Calculation Example

Enclosed crawlspace: 30 feet × 40 feet = 1,200 square feet
Required opening area: 1,200 ÷ 12 = 100 square inches
Openings found:
- Four louvered vents, each 12" × 12" = 144 sq. in. each
- Total: 4 × 144 = 576 sq. inches
- Result: ADEQUATE (576 > 100 required)

### Section F Documentation

Document all openings found. Example entry:

| Location | Type | Dimensions | Elevation | Size (sq. in.) |
|----------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| North rim joist | Louvered vent | 12"W × 12"H | 8.5' above grade | 144 |
| East rim joist | Louvered vent | 12"W × 12"H | 8.3' above grade | 144 |
| South rim joist | Louvered vent | 12"W × 12"H | 8.4' above grade | 144 |
| West rim joist | Louvered vent | 12"W × 12"H | 8.5' above grade | 144 |
| Total adequate opening: 576 sq. inches |

## Machinery and Equipment (M&E) Elevation Documentation

Equipment must be elevated above the BFE or flood-proofed. As an EC professional, you must document where equipment is located and at what elevation.

### Critical Equipment to Document

**Must always document (if present):**
- Furnace/boiler
- Water heater (gas or electric)
- Heat pump
- Air conditioning unit (if lowest component is below BFE)
- Elevator (if present)
- Any other utilities critical for building operation

**Record the LOWEST piece of equipment** if multiple items of same type at different elevations.

### Measurement Protocol

**1. Identify each piece of equipment**
   - Visual inspection throughout building
   - Check mechanical rooms, basements, crawlspaces, attics
   - Ask owner/builder about any equipment not immediately visible

**2. Determine elevation**
   - For each item, identify the lowest point
   - For furnace: lowest point of unit (not top of vent pipe)
   - For water heater: lowest point of tank
   - For A/C: lowest point of unit
   - Measure from grade (same datum as building elevation)

**3. Location documentation**
   - Inside building footprint (interior) or outside (exterior)?
   - If outside: distance and direction from building
   - Photograph showing equipment location and elevation

**4. Compare to BFE**
   - Is equipment above BFE? (preferred)
   - At BFE? (acceptable but not ideal)
   - Below BFE? (requires flood-resistant design or other protection)

### Common Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Equipment Above BFE**
- Furnace at 127 feet, BFE 120 feet → 7 feet ABOVE BFE ✓
- Document and approve

**Scenario 2: Equipment At Grade (Below BFE)**
- Water heater at grade, BFE 120 feet, grade 118 feet → 2 feet BELOW BFE
- Must be flood-proofed or sealed
- Document protection method: "Water heater sealed and on flood-resistant platform"

**Scenario 3: Equipment in Basement (Below BFE)**
- Furnace in basement at 112 feet, BFE 120 feet → 8 feet BELOW BFE
- Building's lowest floor is basement (also at 112 feet)
- Document as being at same elevation as building's lowest floor

### Section E Documentation

Example documentation:

| Equipment | Location | Elevation | Relation to BFE |
|-----------|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Furnace | Interior, mechanical room | 127.2' NAVD | 7.2' above BFE |
| Water heater | Interior, mechanical room | 127.4' NAVD | 7.4' above BFE |
| A/C condenser | Exterior, east side | 118.6' NAVD | 1.4' below BFE |

And note in Section G: "A/C condenser located below BFE but sealed unit on elevated pad; protected from flood damage."

## Photograph Requirements and Best Practices

Photographs are essential documentation. Your EC should include several photos showing:

### Required Photographs

1. **Overall Building View**
   - Shows entire building and immediate surroundings
   - Taken from distance showing building in context
   - Shows building type and elevation relative to grade

2. **Grade Reference Point**
   - Shows the lowest adjacent grade location
   - Clear marking or identifying feature
   - Photograph should show grade surface clearly
   - Include measurement reference (tape measure, ruler in shot if helpful)

3. **Lowest Floor Location**
   - Shows building's lowest floor relative to grade
   - Clear view of foundation type and lowest floor elevation
   - Shows measurement points or reference marks
   - For elevated buildings: clearly shows elevation of support system (piers, pilings)

4. **Each Building Face (for larger buildings)**
   - North, south, east, west exposures
   - Shows terrain and grading on all sides
   - Identifies areas where grade varies significantly

5. **Flood Openings** (if present)
   - Photograph each type of opening
   - Shows location and size
   - Shows elevation relative to grade or structure

6. **Equipment Locations** (if relevant)
   - Photograph equipment outside building or in mechanical room
   - Shows elevation above or below grade
   - Documents protection method if equipment is below BFE

7. **Site Conditions** (if significant)
   - Drainage patterns or site slope
   - Nearby water features or drainage areas
   - Flooding history (if evident)
   - Anything affecting interpretation of elevations

### Photography Best Practices

**Composition:**
- Include reference object (tape measure, person, building element) for scale
- Photograph in daylight with good lighting
- Avoid shadows that obscure important features
- Multiple angles may be necessary for complex sites

**Labeling:**
- Label photos with location, date, and brief description
- Reference photo numbers in narrative sections
- Create photo location map if many photos

**Storage:**
- Digital files clearly named: "Property Address - Photo 1 - Overall View.jpg"
- High resolution (at least 2 MP, preferably higher)
- Include with EC file as scanned attachment or digital copy

**Example Photo Captions:**
- "Photo 1: Overall building view from southwest, showing grade slope and crawlspace elevation"
- "Photo 2: Grade reference point on north side of building; grade elevation 118.4' NAVD 1988"
- "Photo 3: Lowest adjacent grade point (southeast corner); elevation 118.1' NAVD 1988"
- "Photo 4: North side flood openings showing louvered vents in rim joist approximately 8.5' above adjacent grade"

## Section D Certification Responsibilities

Section D is where you certify the building elevation information. This is your professional responsibility and may expose you to liability.

### What You're Certifying

When you sign Section D, you're certifying:
- You personally performed (or supervised) the measurements
- Measurements are accurate to the best of your professional ability
- Methodology is sound and documented
- Building elevation as documented is correct
- You have authority (license) to complete this certification

### Professional Standard

Your certification should meet:
- Standards of your profession (NSPS for surveyors, ASCE for engineers)
- State law requirements for your licensure
- FEMA Publication 467-1 guidance
- Standard of care for elevation measurement work

### Liability Exposure

An incorrect EC can result in:
- **Policyholder loss:** Over or under-charged insurance premiums
- **Insurer loss:** Under-rated policies that result in claim losses
- **Community loss:** Inaccurate floodplain management records
- **Legal action:** Negligence claim against you for inaccurate certification

### Protecting Yourself

1. **Document your methodology thoroughly**
   - Describe equipment used, accuracy achieved
   - Explain vertical control methodology
   - Note any limiting conditions or assumptions

2. **Don't guess or estimate**
   - If you can't accurately measure something, say so
   - Document what you could not access
   - Note limitations in Section G

3. **Verify calculations**
   - Double-check all math before signing
   - Have someone review calculations
   - Use software to verify if available

4. **Use standard terminology**
   - Define "lowest floor" clearly
   - Explain what "lowest adjacent grade" means
   - Use consistent terminology throughout

5. **Document limiting conditions**
   - "Based on information provided by [owner/builder]"
   - "Subject to verification during construction"
   - "Crawlspace not accessed; elevation estimated"

6. **Maintain professional liability insurance**
   - Errors & Omissions (E&O) or Professional Liability
   - Covers claims from inaccurate ECs
   - Verify policy covers flood elevation work

## Common Liability Considerations

### Scenario 1: Building Elevation Later Found Inaccurate

**Situation:** You certified FFH of 5 feet above BFE. After completion, community inspector measures and finds it's actually 3 feet above BFE. Insurance company has been over-charging premiums.

**Exposure:** Claim for negligent measurement.

**Protection:**
- Maintain notes showing your measurement methodology
- Document equipment used and accuracy achieved
- Show photographs supporting your measurements
- If measurements are within standard accuracy for your methods, defense is strong
- Professional liability insurance should cover

### Scenario 2: Equipment Elevation Documentation Incomplete

**Situation:** You documented furnace and water heater elevations but missed a heat pump outside building. Heat pump is below BFE without flood-proofing. Damage occurs during flood.

**Exposure:** Claim that incomplete documentation led to unprotected equipment.

**Protection:**
- Document in Section G that you inspected for all equipment
- List what you found and what conditions were
- If equipment added after your EC completion, document timing
- Your responsibility is what existed during inspection

### Scenario 3: Datum Conversion Error

**Situation:** You converted NAVD 1988 to NGVD 1929 incorrectly, using wrong conversion factor. Building was rated as being above BFE when it's actually below.

**Exposure:** Significant liability—this is a clear professional error.

**Protection:**
- Always use official conversion factors from NOAA or USGS
- Document source of conversion factor in EC
- Double-check conversion calculations
- If uncertain about conversion, request official FEMA conversion or new measurement in correct datum

### Scenario 4: Inaccessible Crawlspace

**Situation:** Crawlspace is inaccessible so you estimated floor elevation from exterior observations. Later, building owner enters crawlspace and finds floor much lower than estimated.

**Exposure:** Claim that estimation was unreasonable or inadequate.

**Protection:**
- Clearly document in Section G: "Crawlspace not accessible; floor elevation estimated based on [specific methodology]"
- Note the limitation and recommend verification
- Document what you actually measured vs. what was estimated
- Recommend community inspection to verify
- Ask client whether they want to improve access or accept estimation

### Scenario 5: Building Modified After EC Completion

**Situation:** You complete EC showing basement lowest floor. Later, builder raises foundation and fills basement. Floor is now much higher. Old EC is used for rating calculations.

**Exposure:** Minimal if timing is clear. Old EC still accurately reflects conditions when completed.

**Protection:**
- Date your EC clearly with completion date
- Document building conditions as of that date
- If building substantially modified after, new EC should be completed
- Your certification is valid as of completion date; not responsible for post-completion modifications

## Realistic Fee Discussion

EC fees vary widely based on location, complexity, and market conditions.

### Typical Fee Range

**Simple residential building (single story, straightforward elevation):**
- $500–$1,200
- Survey time: 2–3 hours
- Office time: 1–2 hours
- Travel and overhead

**Moderate complexity (multi-story, crawlspace, slope, equipment documentation):**
- $1,200–$2,500
- Survey time: 3–5 hours
- Office time: 2–4 hours
- Additional field work, documentation

**High complexity (multiple structures, limited access, datum conversion, extensive documentation):**
- $2,500–$5,000+
- Survey time: 6+ hours
- Office time: 4+ hours
- Specialized equipment, advanced analysis

### Factors Affecting Price

- **Distance to property:** Longer travel = higher cost
- **Building complexity:** Multiple stories, varied elevation = higher cost
- **Site conditions:** Difficult terrain, crawlspace access, drainage issues = higher cost
- **Documentation requirements:** Photos, site plan, detailed report = higher cost
- **Market rates:** Regional variation in survey/engineering rates
- **Equipment needs:** GPS vs. basic measurement affects cost
- **Timeline:** Rush service typically costs 25–50% premium

### Setting Fees

Price competitively while protecting your costs:
- Don't undercut quality with unrealistic low fees
- Complex properties may not be profitable at bare-minimum rates
- Consider insurance and liability exposure in pricing
- Charge appropriate premium for rush/weekend work
- Include all documentation, site plan, and photos in fee

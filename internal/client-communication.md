# Client Communication Templates

## Overview

This document provides email templates and talking points for all phases of an Elevation Certificate engagement. Customize the language, pricing, timelines, and contact details to match your firm's operations and regional standards.

## Template 1: Initial Inquiry Response

**Scenario**: A homeowner, insurance agent, or property manager contacts your firm requesting an EC.

**Use Case**: Reply to phone message, email, or online inquiry form.

---

### Email Template: What Is an Elevation Certificate?

```
Subject: Elevation Certificate Inquiry — [Your Firm Name]

Dear [Client Name],

Thank you for reaching out regarding an Elevation Certificate (EC) for your property at
[address]. We're happy to help.

WHAT IS AN ELEVATION CERTIFICATE?

An Elevation Certificate is a technical document prepared by a licensed engineer or surveyor
that documents the elevation of your building relative to flood levels in your area. It is:

• Required by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) as part of the National Flood
  Insurance Program (NFIP)
• Used by your insurance company to determine flood insurance premiums
• A permanent record of your building's elevation at the time of certification
• Valid indefinitely, unless you modify the building structure

WHY DO YOU NEED ONE?

Common reasons include:
• Applying for flood insurance rate reduction if your home is above the base flood elevation
• Obtaining a Lender's Determined Elevation (required by some mortgage lenders)
• Documenting building elevation for future construction or renovations
• Complying with community or insurance company requirements

WHAT WE WILL DO

Our team will:
1. Gather information about your property and the surrounding flood zone
2. Visit your site to measure your building's elevation relative to established reference points
3. Document crawlspace or garage details (if applicable)
4. Take photographs of the foundation and any flood-related features
5. Complete the FEMA Elevation Certificate form with accurate technical data
6. Provide you and your insurance agent with a professional, sealed copy

WHAT WE NEED FROM YOU

To get started, please provide:
• Full property address and legal description (if available)
• Reason for needing the EC (rate reduction, lender requirement, other)
• Insurance company and policy number (helpful but not required)
• Preferred dates and times for an on-site visit (typically takes 30–60 minutes)
• Any access restrictions (gate codes, pets, locked areas)

To schedule your site visit, please reply with:

### Talking Points (for Phone Conversation)

When speaking with a client by phone, cover these points in a natural, conversational tone:

**Opening**:
- "Thanks for calling. An Elevation Certificate is a technical document we prepare that shows how high your building is compared to flood levels in your area."

**Why They Need It**:
- "Insurance companies use ECs to calculate flood insurance premiums. If your home is above the flood level, you could qualify for a lower rate."
- "Some lenders require it as part of the mortgage process."
- "It's a permanent record of your building's elevation, which is useful if you ever renovate or make changes."

**Our Process**:
- "We'll visit your property, measure your home's elevation using surveying equipment, document the crawlspace or garage if you have one, and take a few photographs."
- "The whole site visit usually takes 30 to 60 minutes."
- "After we finish the fieldwork, we'll prepare the FEMA form in our office and send you a sealed copy."

**Timeline**:
- "We typically need 2 to 3 weeks from the site visit to delivery, depending on our current workload."
- "Would next week work for a site visit, or are you thinking further out?"

**Cost**:
- "For a standard residential property, we charge [price range]. Final cost depends on how complex the building is and whether we need to do any special surveying."
- "I can give you a more exact quote once we discuss your specific property."

**Access**:
- "We'll need access to your crawlspace or garage if you have one, and access to the interior if possible."
- "Do you have any pets, or is there anything we should know before we visit?"
- "The site visit is pretty straightforward—we're not invasive, and we'll be respectful of your home."

## Template 4: Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

**Scenario**: Create a one-page FAQ document to include with initial inquiry responses or post on your website.

---

### FAQ Document

```
ELEVATION CERTIFICATE FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Q: How long is an Elevation Certificate valid?
A: An EC is valid indefinitely unless you make physical changes to the structure of your
building. If you add a second story, raise the building on a new foundation, extend the
footprint, or make other structural modifications, a new EC may be required by your insurance
company or lender. Changes like painting, re-roofing, or finishing a basement do not require
a new EC (they don't change the building elevation).

Q: Will an Elevation Certificate lower my flood insurance premiums?
A: It might. If your home is built higher than the base flood elevation for your area, your
insurance rates might be reduced. The amount of the reduction depends on:
• How much higher your home is above the base flood elevation
• Your insurance company's rating rules
• Your current insurance policy and flood zone

Submit your completed EC to your insurance agent to find out if you qualify for a discount.

Q: Do I need a professional survey to get an EC, or can I do it myself?
A: It depends on your situation:

• **Section H (Self-Certification)**: You can self-certify the elevation of your lowest floor
  if you have building permits or construction records that show the floor height. Many homeowners
  use this method for a lower cost.

• **Section C (Professional Certification)**: If you want the most accurate elevation data, or
  if you need to apply for a LOMA (Letter of Map Amendment) with FEMA, you need a licensed
  professional (engineer or surveyor) to perform Section C. This is what we provide.

A professional certified EC (Section C) is required for some insurance situations and is the
gold standard for accuracy.

Q: What's the difference between an Elevation Certificate and a survey?
A: They're related but different:

• **Elevation Certificate**: A technical document that measures your building's elevation
  relative to flood levels. It doesn't establish property boundaries. It focuses on vertical
  measurements (how high your building is).

• **Boundary Survey or Property Survey**: A document that establishes the legal boundaries of
  your property using boundary monuments, deeds, and recorded measurements. It typically
  includes elevation data but is broader in scope.

An EC is a specialized vertical measurement; a survey is a broader boundary and site document.

Q: Is an Elevation Certificate required by law?
A: Not always, but:

• **Flood Insurance**: If you have a mortgage and your property is in a Special Flood Hazard
  Area (SFHA), your lender must ensure you have flood insurance, but they don't always require
  a professional EC.

• **Building Permits**: Some communities require an EC as part of the building permit process
  for new construction or major renovations.

• **Insurance Discounts**: To qualify for a premium reduction, your insurance company may
  request a professional EC.

Check with your lender, insurance company, and local building department to confirm requirements
for your property.

Q: How much does an Elevation Certificate cost?
A: Cost varies by property complexity and location:

• **Standard residential property (slab or simple crawlspace)**: [Price range, e.g.,
  "$300–$600"]
• **More complex properties (multi-story, basement, attached garage)**: [Price range, e.g.,
  "$600–$1,200"]
• **Heavily elevated properties or special features**: [Price range, e.g., "$1,000+"]

Travel distance may add to the cost. Contact us for a specific quote for your property.

Q: How long does it take to get an Elevation Certificate?
A: Typical timeline:

• **Schedule site visit**: 1–2 weeks after request
• **Site visit**: 30–60 minutes at your property
• **Office work and quality review**: 1–2 weeks
• **Total time to delivery**: 2–4 weeks depending on workload

We can often expedite for an additional fee if needed.

Q: What do I need to provide for an Elevation Certificate?
A: For the site visit, please provide:

• **Access**: Your home must be accessible (interior, crawlspace, garage, exterior)
• **Property information**: Address, legal description if available
• **Building permits** (optional): If you have them, they're helpful for verification
• **FIRM information** (optional): We can pull this, but it speeds things up if you have
  your flood zone handy
• **Insurance info** (optional): Policy number is helpful but not required

That's it! We'll handle the rest.

Q: What happens if I can't access my crawlspace during the visit?
A: If your crawlspace is inaccessible (sealed, flooded, or locked), we'll document the
limitation in the EC and note it in our comments. We can estimate some data or, if critical
measurements can't be completed, the EC may be incomplete or require a follow-up visit.

It's best to arrange access before our visit.

Q: Can you submit my Elevation Certificate to FEMA or my insurance company?
A: Yes! We can submit on your behalf if you'd like:

• **Insurance company**: We can mail or email a copy directly to your agent or insurer
• **Local floodplain administrator**: We can deliver to your community official
• **FEMA**: We typically don't submit directly, but you or your agent can

Let us know your preferences, and we'll handle distribution.

Q: What if I have a manufactured home or mobile home?
A: We can prepare an EC for a manufactured home. We'll measure the elevation of the actual
floor structure (above any skirting). Skirting does not count as a permanent enclosure, so we
document it separately. Let us know when you contact us so we can plan accordingly.

Q: What's the difference between NGVD 29 and NAVD 88?
A: These are two different vertical reference systems (datums) used to establish elevation:

• **NGVD 29**: National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929 (older system)
• **NAVD 88**: North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (newer, more accurate)

Your FIRM might reference one or the other. We'll use the correct datum for your EC and, if
needed, convert between them using published NOAA/USGS factors. We document any conversion in
the EC.

Q: My property is very flat. Will an Elevation Certificate still be useful?
A: Yes. Even on a flat lot, we'll establish the elevation of your building foundation relative
to the BFE. Flat properties are often easier to measure and may qualify for bigger insurance
discounts if they're well above BFE.

Q: What should I do with my EC after I receive it?
A: Keep it for your records and:

1. **Provide to your insurance agent**: See if you qualify for a premium reduction
2. **Keep with home documentation**: Store safely (PDF backup is helpful)
3. **Note the date**: Remember that it's valid indefinitely unless you modify the building
4. **Use for future sales/refinancing**: Potential buyers or lenders may request it

Don't lose it—getting another one could require another site visit and cost.

Q: What if my building has a split-level or sunken room?
A: We'll measure the lowest floor elevation in your home, even if it's a sunken room or split-
level space. We'll document the building's irregular shape in the EC and explain any special
conditions in our comments. This ensures the most accurate representation of your property.

Q: Do you work with my homeowners association or property manager?
A: Yes! We work with HOAs, property managers, and multi-family property owners frequently. If
your property is managed by an association or company, they can request an EC and authorize
the site visit on your behalf. We'll provide copies to all parties as needed.

FINDING 1: YOUR HOME IS BELOW THE BASE FLOOD ELEVATION

**Measurement**: Your lowest floor elevation is [X.X] feet
**Base Flood Elevation (BFE)**: [Y.Y] feet
**Difference**: [X.X – Y.Y] = [result] feet BELOW BFE

This means your home is located in the Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) and is vulnerable to
flooding during a base flood event.

**What this means for insurance**:
• Flood insurance is required by most mortgage lenders
• Your property is in a higher-risk zone, so premiums will reflect that risk
• An EC won't reduce your premiums in this case—it documents your actual risk level

**Options to consider**:
• Pursue a LOMA (Letter of Map Amendment) with FEMA if you believe the FIRM mapping is
  incorrect
• Consider flood mitigation: raising the building, adding flood vents, installing sump pumps,
  etc.
• Ensure you have adequate flood insurance coverage
• Budget for potential flood damage or mitigation costs if you keep the property

I'm happy to discuss these options further.

---

FINDING 2: MISSING OR INADEQUATE FLOOD VENTS

**Observation**: Your crawlspace has [description, e.g., "only one flood vent on the foundation,
when two are recommended for non-engineered enclosures"].

**Standard**: FEMA and ASCE 24 recommend at least 2 flood vents per side of an enclosure, with
a minimum net open area of 1 square inch per 1 square foot of crawlspace.

**Your property**:
• Crawlspace area: [Sq ft]
• Vent openings: [Number]
• Net open area: [Sq in]
• Requirement: [Sq in]
• Status: [Compliant / Inadequate]

**What this means**:
• During flooding, water pressure could build up inside your crawlspace, putting stress on
  the foundation
• Flood vents allow water to equalize pressure and minimize structural damage

**Recommendations**:
• Add additional flood vents to meet ASCE 24 standards
• Install engineered flood vents or louvers (more expensive but more reliable)
• Have a contractor evaluate the cost and feasibility

This is not a critical issue for the EC itself, but it's worth considering if you're planning
renovations or want to improve flood resilience.

---

FINDING 3: LOW-ELEVATION MECHANICAL EQUIPMENT

**Observation**: Your outdoor air conditioning unit is at elevation [X.X] feet, which is
[distance] below your lowest floor.

**Risk**: During a flood event reaching your lowest floor elevation, this outdoor equipment
will likely be submerged and damaged, leading to costly repairs or replacement.

**Mitigation options**:
• Elevate the HVAC unit pad on a pedestal or concrete pad (typical cost: [estimate])
• Move the unit to a higher location if feasible
• Install a water-resistant cover or flood barrier (temporary solution)
• Budget for replacement costs if you accept the risk

Discuss this with your HVAC contractor and insurance agent.

4. Keep your EC for your records

## Template 6: Denial or Issue Resolution

**Scenario**: A client is disappointed by findings (e.g., home is below BFE, no discount qualifies) or questions the accuracy of your work.

---

### Email/Phone Talking Points: Addressing Disappointment

```
CONVERSATION STRUCTURE FOR DIFFICULT FINDINGS

Situation: Client is disappointed that EC shows home below BFE or doesn't qualify for insurance
discount.

Opening (empathetic):
"I understand this may not be the result you were hoping for. Let me explain what the
measurements show and what your options are."

Explain the data (factual, professional):
"Based on our field measurements and FIRM data, your home's lowest floor is at [elevation],
and FEMA's base flood elevation for your area is [elevation]. This means your home is [X]
feet below the flood level."

Clarify what it means (plain language):
"That doesn't mean you'll definitely flood—it depends on the actual flood event. But statistically,
your home is in a higher-risk area, so insurance premiums reflect that risk."

Offer solutions (proactive):
"If you believe our measurements are incorrect, we can do a re-check. Or, if you think the
FIRM flood level is wrong, we can help you pursue a LOMA with FEMA. Another option is to
consider raising your building or adding flood protection."

Reassure on accuracy (build confidence):
"I want to be clear: our measurements are accurate and based on standard surveying practices.
We used [equipment] and tied to an established benchmark. We've reviewed the EC through quality
control, and everything checks out."

Close professionally (supportive):
"I know this isn't ideal, but having an accurate EC protects you in the long run. It shows
your actual risk level, which helps you make informed decisions about insurance, mitigation,
and future property decisions."
```

## Customization Guidance

Each firm should adapt these templates by:

1. **Adding Your Contact Information**: Firm name, phone, email, address, license info
2. **Adjusting Pricing and Timeline**: Use your actual cost ranges and typical turnaround
3. **Regional Details**: Mention common building types, datums, and FIRM panels in your service area
4. **Brand Voice**: Make the language match your firm's professional tone and style
5. **Specific Equipment or Methods**: Reference the surveying tools and QC processes you actually use
6. **Legal Disclaimers**: Add any state-specific language or liability disclaimers your firm's attorney recommends
7. **Insurance Company References**: Mention local insurance companies or national carriers you work with regularly
8. **Mitigation Partnerships**: Reference local contractors or mitigation specialists if you have referral relationships

---

## Notes for Implementation

- **Digital vs. Print**: Most of these templates work well as email or PDF. Consider a printed
  brochure version of the FAQ for walk-in clients or real estate agents.
- **Personalization**: Customize each template with client name, property address, and specific
  findings before sending.
- **Accessibility**: Use clear language, define technical terms, and avoid jargon when possible.
- **Follow-Up**: Set reminders to follow up 2–3 weeks after delivery to gather feedback and
  resolve any issues.
- **Social Proof**: Consider adding client testimonials or case studies once you've completed
  several ECs.

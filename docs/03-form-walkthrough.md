# Form Walkthrough: FEMA FF-206-FY-22-152 (2023 Edition)

## Overview of Form Structure

The Elevation Certificate form consists of multiple sections, photographs, and appendices. Each section serves a specific purpose and is completed by different parties depending on authorization and building characteristics. Understanding what goes in each section, why it matters, and what common errors to avoid will significantly improve the quality and acceptance of submitted ECs.

The form is organized logically: first property identification (Section A), then FIRM information (Section B), then building elevations (Section C or E/H depending on zone type), then certifications (Sections D, F, I), community information (Section G), and finally building photographs.

---

## Section A: Property and Owner Information

**Completed by:** Property owner or applicant (sometimes floodplain manager)

Section A establishes the identity and fundamental characteristics of the property and building.

**Owner Name and Address:** Self-explanatory, but verify that the name matches property records and the mailing address is current. If the property has changed ownership since the EC was initiated, update the name accordingly.

**Property Address or Legal Description:** The street address and zip code. If the property has no street address (rural properties), provide the legal description from the deed or tax record. Precision matters; an ambiguous address can result in the EC being applied to the wrong building.

**Latitude and Longitude:** Most communities now require these to precisely locate the property on digital maps. As discussed in the Authorization section, these do not need to come from a surveyor. However, they should be accurate to at least the nearest second of arc (roughly 100 feet). Check your entry against the FIRM online or Google Maps to confirm the coordinates place the building in the correct location.

**Building Use:** Select from the provided options: single-family dwelling, multi-unit residential, non-residential, etc. This classification affects some rating methodologies and community permit requirements. Be precise—a duplex is "multi-unit residential," not "single-family."

**Building Diagram Number:** This is critical. The nine building diagrams (1A, 1B, 2A, 2B, 3–8) represent different structural types. Selecting the wrong diagram leads to incorrect elevation interpretation. The diagram should match the building's actual construction: if the building is elevated on piers, it must be Diagram 5, 6, or 7, not Diagram 1 (slab-on-grade). See "[Building Diagrams Explained](04-building-diagrams.md)" for detailed guidance.

**Crawlspace or Enclosure Details:** If the building has a crawlspace, basement, or enclosed area below the lowest occupiable floor, document the details:

- Whether there is a crawlspace or enclosure
- The construction type (concrete slab, dirt floor, gravel, etc.)
- **Flood opening information:** For buildings in A zones with enclosures, FEMA requires flood openings (typically flood vents or breakaway walls) that allow flood water to flow freely into and out of the enclosure. This section documents whether such openings exist and their location. If flood openings are present, their total area must comply with FEMA standards (typically 1 square foot per 150 square feet of enclosed area, with specific area calculations).
- For crawlspaces, note whether utilities (mechanical, electrical, HVAC) are located in the crawlspace—this affects which elevation measures are taken.

**Attached Garage Details:** Garages attached to the main building often have a lower finished floor (the concrete slab may be at or below the lowest living area floor). Document:

- Whether a garage is attached
- The type of door (single, double, roll-up, etc.)
- Whether the garage is finished (storage, living space) or unfinished (parking only)

Garages finished for living space must meet the same elevation requirements as the rest of the building. Garages used only for parking may have different requirements depending on zone and community standards. This distinction is essential for proper regulatory interpretation.

## Section B: FIRM (Flood Insurance Rate Map) Information

**Completed by:** Property owner or floodplain manager (often assisted by both)

Section B documents the official flood hazard information for the property's location according to the current effective FIRM. This is the baseline against which the building's actual elevations are compared.

**Community Name and FIPS Code:** The incorporated city or town name, or the county name for unincorporated areas. The FIPS code (Federal Information Processing Standards) uniquely identifies the community. This ensures the EC is matched to the correct community's regulations.

**County Name:** For navigation and verification.

**FIRM Panel Number:** FIRMs are published in panels (typically showing a few square miles each). The panel number is used to locate the specific map and ensure you're referencing the correct version.

**Flood Zone:** The zone designation from the FIRM: AE, A, VE, V, B, C, X (shaded or unshaded), or other designation. If the property is in Zone X (low-risk or moderate-risk), certain EC sections become optional.

**Base Flood Elevation (BFE):** If a BFE is mapped (as in AE, A1–A30, VE, and V zones), enter the elevation in feet (NAVD 88 is the standard datum, though older maps may reference other datums). If no BFE is mapped (Zone A without specific BFE designation, some AO zones), leave this blank or enter "not applicable" as directed.

**Basis for BFE:** Indicate the source: from the FIRM, from a FEMA flood study (letter), from a local flood study, or from other official source. This context helps downstream users understand the reliability and vintage of the baseline.

**Vertical Datum:** Always verify the datum. NAVD 88 (North American Vertical Datum of 1988) is current; NGVD 29 (National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929) is older. Maps and regulatory requirements increasingly require NAVD 88. If your building's measured elevations are in NAVD 29 (because that's the datum of the local benchmark), you must convert to NAVD 88 for the official EC. Document the conversion methodology clearly.

**Coastal Barrier Resources System (CBRS) and OPA:** If the property is within a designated CBRS area or Otherwise Protected Area, check "yes." These designations have insurance implications: CBRS properties may not be eligible for standard NFIP policies and may have to purchase coverage through insurer-of-last-resort programs. CBRS areas are often undeveloped or are being managed to preserve coastal ecosystems.

**Limit of Moderate Wave Action (LiMWA):** In coastal V zones, the LiMWA indicates the inland boundary where wave action decreases to moderate levels. If the building is in a V zone, determine whether it is seaward or landward of the LiMWA—this affects structural requirements and may affect rating. LiMWA information is typically available from the community's floodplain manager or from FEMA's flood map viewer.

## Section C: Building Elevation Information (Survey-Required)

**Completed by:** Licensed surveyor, engineer, or architect authorized by state law

Section C is where detailed elevation measurements are documented. This is the heart of a professional EC.

**Description of Surveyed/Measured Points:** The professional documents how the building's elevations were measured: "tape measure from benchmark to first floor, verified with level," or "RTK GPS survey with local datum tie," or similar. This describes the methodology.

**Benchmark Used:** The official or unofficial reference elevation point used for measurement. For maximum credibility, this should be a FEMA-published benchmark or a benchmark published by the local community. If a local benchmark is not available, a surveyor may establish a temporary benchmark or tie to a natural feature (edge of riverbank, top of a known structure) provided it is adequately documented.

**Datum:** The vertical reference system. Confirm this matches (or is convertible to) the datum required by the community and insurance company—typically NAVD 88.

**Elevation Measurements a through h:** Each row represents a specific elevation point:

- **a) Lowest floor of the building:** The elevation of the lowest enclosed living space in A zones, or the lowest structural floor in V zones. For most residential buildings, this is the main living area's floor level. For buildings with basements, it is the basement floor; for elevated buildings, it is the lowest enclosed level.

- **b) Next higher floor:** If there is a second floor, document its elevation.

- **c) Lowest horizontal structural member in A zones:** In A zones, crawlspaces are permitted, and utilities can be located there. The elevation of the lowest structural part (rim joist, beam, or lowest part of the floor system) must be documented separately from the lowest enclosed floor.

- **d) Garage slab elevation:** Separate from the main building if the garage floor is at a different level (which it often is).

- **e) Mechanical and electrical equipment elevation:** M&E that is part of the building systems and could be affected by flooding. Large HVAC units, electrical panels, furnaces located in unprotected areas must be elevated above the BFE in many zones, or their elevation must be documented if they are wet-floodable.

- **f) Lowest adjacent grade (LAG):** The elevation of the finished ground directly adjacent to the building's lowest wall, averaged around the perimeter. This is critical for interpreting the building's exposure to water. If LAG is well below the lowest floor, the building is clearly elevated; if LAG is close to the lowest floor, the building may be slab-on-grade or elevated minimally.

- **g) Highest adjacent grade (HAG):** For properties with sloped terrain, the highest finished grade at any adjacent point, useful for understanding grading patterns.

- **h) Finished LAG at deck/stairs/ramp:** If the building has decks, exterior stairs, or ramps, their slab elevations are documented separately. Decks are often lower than the main floor and must also meet elevation requirements in flood-prone zones.

**Lowest Floor Elevation (Most Critical for Insurance):** This is the final calculation or measurement showing the lowest occupied floor's elevation. For buildings in A zones, this is compared to the BFE to determine freeboard (how many feet above flood risk). For insurance rating, this is the First Floor Height.

## Section D: Surveyor/Engineer/Architect Certification

**Completed by:** The licensed professional who completed Section C

Section D is the professional's seal and signature. It certifies that the professional measured or otherwise determined the elevations documented in Section C, that they did so in accordance with FEMA guidance and professional standards, and that the information is true and correct to the best of their knowledge.

The professional must include:

- Their printed name and professional license number
- Their state of licensure
- A current, valid professional seal (stamp)
- Their signature (original, not photocopied; some communities now accept digital signatures)
- The date of signing

An unsigned, unsealed, or expired-seal Section D makes the entire Section C uncertifiable. Communities and insurers will reject it. Before submitting, verify seal validity.

## Section E: Building Measurement Information (No Survey)

**Completed by:** Property owner or any person; no survey required

Section E is an alternative to Section C for properties where a detailed survey-based elevation is not needed. It is used for:

- Zone AO buildings (areas of shallow flooding)
- Zone AR or AR/AO buildings
- Zone A without a mapped BFE

For these properties, the EC documents the building's construction and elevation relationship using simpler methods. A property owner can measure with a tape measure and estimate to the nearest foot; a professional can measure with greater precision. Section E asks for:

- Building construction type and materials
- Number of floors
- Finished floor elevation measured from grade (e.g., "24 inches above finished grade")
- Utilities location
- Basic diagram indicating the building's profile

The standard required for Section E is considerably lower than Section C. If a measurement is off by a foot or two, it is usually acceptable, whereas Section C measurements should be to the nearest 0.1 foot.

---

## Section F: Property Owner Certification for Section E

**Completed by:** Property owner or authorized representative

When a property owner completes Section E, they sign Section F certifying that they provided accurate information. This is a sworn statement; false certifications can have legal consequences.

## Section G: Community Information

**Completed by:** Community floodplain manager or local official

Section G provides official community context:

- **Permit Information:** Date of last permit issued for the property, permit type, and cost. This helps verify that construction occurred.
- **As-Built Elevation Data:** If the community has as-built or other elevation records, they may be noted here.
- **Community's BFE for the Property:** The official base flood elevation according to community records (which should match the FIRM, but sometimes communities have more recent data).
- **Community Minimum Elevation:** Many communities adopt minimum elevation standards that exceed the BFE—e.g., "first floor must be 1 foot above BFE." This is documented here.
- **Variances or Waivers:** If the property has been granted a variance allowing construction below the community minimum, it is documented here.

Many ECs are submitted without Section G completed. Property owners should request that their community's floodplain manager fill this out and provide a copy. It strengthens the EC's completeness and ensures the community's official position is part of the record.

## Section H: First Floor Height (No Survey)

**Completed by:** Property owner, authorized representative, or local floodplain official

Section H documents the building's First Floor Height specifically for insurance rating. Unlike Section C, no survey is required. A property owner measuring their own FFH might:

1. Measure the height of the first floor slab or finished floor above the lowest adjacent grade using a tape measure and level
2. Document the measurement (e.g., "36 inches above grade")
3. Enter the FFH (e.g., if the lot elevation is 10 feet NAVD 88 and the floor is 36 inches above grade, the FFH is 10.0 + 3.0 = 13.0 feet NAVD 88)

For precision work, a professional measuring FFH will:

1. Establish a local control point (often a benchmark)
2. Measure the building's floor elevation to the nearest 0.1 foot
3. Document the methodology clearly
4. Include a checkbox indicating "certified by surveyor/engineer/architect: Yes/No"

Section H includes a field asking: **"First Floor Height certified by a surveyor/engineer/architect? Yes/No"** This allows property owners to indicate whether a professional was involved. If "Yes," the professional's name is included. If "No," the measurement is self-reported.

For insurance purposes, many insurers accept self-reported FFH, particularly for modest residential properties in lower-risk zones. However, for properties seeking a LOMA or involved in dispute resolution, a professionally certified FFH carries more weight.

---

## Section I: Property Owner Certification for Section H

**Completed by:** Property owner or authorized representative

Similar to Section F, Section I certifies that the FFH information in Section H is accurate. This is a sworn statement.

## Building Photographs

**Completed by:** Property owner or professional preparing the EC

The EC requires building photographs documenting the structure's external condition and particularly its foundation, flood openings, and enclosures. **Minimum of 2 photographs; best practice is 4 (one per side).**

Good EC photographs show:

- **Foundation and lowest floor:** A clear view of how the building meets the ground, whether it is slab-on-grade, elevated on piers, or in/out of a crawlspace
- **Flood openings (if applicable):** Close-up views of flood vents, breakaway walls, or other openings that allow water to flow in and out of enclosures
- **Utilities and equipment:** If mechanical or electrical equipment is located in an enclosure, photographed to show its elevation relative to the enclosure floor
- **Deck and stairs:** If exterior stairs or ramps are part of the structure, showing their relationship to the main floor and grade
- **Surrounding terrain:** Wide shots showing the building's relationship to adjacent grade and any nearby waterways or drainage features

Photographs should be dated (or at minimum, clearly dated in the EC submission), labeled (e.g., "North elevation," "East side flood opening"), and clear enough that viewers can discern the structural details. Blurry, distant, or night photographs weaken the EC's credibility.

## Common Errors and Prevention

**Mismatched Building Diagram:** Selecting Diagram 1 (slab-on-grade) for an elevated building or Diagram 5 (elevated on piers) for a basement building leads to misinterpretation of elevation data. Carefully match the diagram to the actual construction.

**Inconsistent Datums:** If the FIRM uses NAVD 88 and your measured elevations are in NGVD 29, the EC will be confusing and unusable. Ensure all elevations are in the same datum, or document conversion clearly.

**Missing Professional Seal:** Section C without a current, valid seal is not legally binding. Before final submission, confirm seal validity.

**Ambiguous or Missing Lowest Floor Definition:** For buildings with complex foundations (split-levels, multiple basements), clearly indicate which floor is the "lowest" for purposes of the EC. If there is ambiguity, document it in writing.

**Incomplete Section G:** Even if not completed by the community at time of submission, follow up to ensure Section G is added to the final record.

**Poor Quality or Missing Photographs:** Inadequate photos invite rejection. Invest time in clear, labeled, dated photos showing key features.

**Inconsistency Between Sections:** If Section C shows a lowest floor elevation of 12.5 feet but Section H shows 13.2 feet, explain why. Small differences are normal (rounding, different measurement points), but large gaps suggest error.

## Quality Control Before Submission

Before submitting an EC:

1. **Verify all sections are complete** for the building type and zone
2. **Check consistency** between building diagram, elevation measurements, and photographs
3. **Confirm professional credentials** (if applicable): valid license, current seal, signature and date present
4. **Verify datum** throughout the form matches community requirements (typically NAVD 88)
5. **Review photographs** for clarity and accuracy
6. **Run the EC past your community's floodplain manager** for early feedback (many will review drafts informally)

Taking time for quality control before submission reduces delays and rejection rates.

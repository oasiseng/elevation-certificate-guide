# Risk Rating 2.0 and the Role of Elevation Certificates

## The Shift from Zone-Based to Property-Specific Rating

Before 2021, the NFIP used zone-based rating. Your insurance premium depended primarily on which zone your property was mapped in (Special Flood Hazard Area, Zone X, etc.). All properties in a zone paid roughly the same rate, regardless of actual individual risk factors.

Risk Rating 2.0, fully implemented in 2023, fundamentally changed this model. The NFIP now rates each property individually based on that specific property's characteristics. Two houses on the same block, both in the same zone, may pay different premiums because their individual risk profiles differ.

This shift created a new value proposition for Elevation Certificates: they are no longer compliance documents primarily, but can be financial documents that reduce premiums by providing accurate first floor height data.

## The Rating Factors in Risk Rating 2.0

FEMA's system evaluates approximately 40 variables per property, but the most material factors affecting most residential policies are:

1. **Proximity to Flood Source** (distance and direction from nearest waterway/hazard area)
2. **Base Flood Elevation** (the regulatory flood elevation for the property)
3. **First Floor Height** (the elevation of the lowest floor—critical factor)
4. **Occupancy Type** (single-family dwelling, multi-family, commercial, etc.)
5. **Construction Type** (masonry, wood frame, concrete, manufactured home)
6. **Number of Stories** (above and below BFE)
7. **Foundation Type** (slab, basement, crawlspace, elevated on pilings)
8. **Cost to Rebuild** (replacement value of structure)
9. **Claims History** (past flood losses on the property)
10. **Special Characteristics** (Increased Cost of Compliance coverage, prior assessments, elevation changes)

Of these, First Floor Height has become one of the most sensitive inputs. The rating system was designed to heavily reward elevation—buildings with higher first floors relative to BFE receive dramatically lower premiums.

## How FEMA Initially Determines First Floor Height

When NFIP underwrites a policy, the system does not automatically have an Elevation Certificate on file. Instead, FEMA uses a multi-step hierarchy to estimate FFH:

### Step 1: Application Information
- FEMA may ask the applicant: "Is your building elevated?"
- Application checkboxes may indicate: "Elevated on pilings," "Slab," "Basement," etc.
- The applicant's answer defaults FFH estimates

### Step 2: Automated Datasets
- FEMA/NFIP uses proprietary datasets that include LiDAR data, assessor records, building permit records, and third-party elevation data
- These datasets assign an estimated FFH to the property based on community-wide geographic data
- Accuracy varies widely: in some communities, estimates are within 1-2 feet; in others, they may be 5-10 feet off

### Step 3: Community Records
- The NFIP may query the community's FEMA-approved floodplain management records (maps, surveys, prior ECs)
- If an old EC or community record exists, that data may be used

### Step 4: Default Assumptions
- If no information is available, FEMA applies default assumptions based on zone and construction type
- Example: properties in a Special Flood Hazard Area default to "First Floor at or below BFE" (FFH = 0 feet relative to BFE)
- This assumption is conservative but often incorrect

**The Critical Point:** FEMA's initial FFH estimate is frequently wrong. It may be too conservative (estimating the building is lower than it actually is, resulting in higher premiums) or occasionally too optimistic. An accurate EC corrects this assumption.

## How an Accurate EC Reduces Your Premium

When an Elevation Certificate is submitted to the NFIP, the system compares the new FFH data in the EC against the FFH that was used to rate the policy initially.

**Scenario: The EC Shows FFH is Higher Than Assumed**
- Initial rating: FEMA assumed FFH = 2 feet above BFE (estimated from LiDAR)
- EC submitted: Professional survey shows FFH = 6 feet above BFE
- Result: Significant premium reduction because the building is actually more elevated than the dataset suggested
- Reduction applies to the policy and typically continues for future renewal years (assuming no building changes)

**Scenario: The EC Confirms FFH**
- Initial rating: FEMA estimated FFH = 5 feet; EC confirms FFH = 5 feet
- Result: No change (premium reflects accurate data already)
- But the EC provides documentation and certainty

**Scenario: The EC Shows FFH is Lower Than Assumed**
- Initial rating: FEMA estimated FFH = 6 feet; EC shows FFH = 3 feet
- Result: Premium adjustment upward
- (This is less common but can occur if LiDAR was inaccurate or the building has been modified)

## FFH Discount Examples: Real Numbers from FEMA

FEMA publishes discount tables for Risk Rating 2.0. The actual discount depends on foundation type, occupancy, and other factors, but here are representative examples for a single-family home on a slab/basement/crawlspace foundation in a High-Risk area (Zone AE):

| First Floor Height | Discount vs. BFE | Annual Premium Example |
|---|---|---|
| 0 feet (at or below BFE) | 0% | $1,500 (baseline) |
| 2 feet above BFE | 14.2% | $1,287 |
| 4 feet above BFE | 28.4% | $1,074 |
| 6 feet above BFE | 37.8% | $932 |
| 8 feet above BFE | 41.7% | $876 |
| 10 feet above BFE | 43.5% | $847 |

**Key Observations:**
- Each additional foot of elevation yields a discount, but the discount curve flattens at higher elevations
- The biggest jump is from 0 to 4 feet (28.4% savings)
- An EC showing FFH = 4 feet vs. the default FFH = 0 feet could save nearly $500/year
- Over a 30-year mortgage, that's $15,000 in cumulative savings
- This is why an EC has become a financial document, not just a compliance tool

## ECs Are Optional but Often Beneficial

Under Risk Rating 2.0, **Elevation Certificates are optional for insurance rating**. The NFIP will rate a property without one, using its dataset estimates and conservative defaults.

However:
- **An EC is beneficial if it shows higher FFH than FEMA's default assumption**
- **An EC is also valuable as documentation of your property's baseline status** (useful if you later modify the building or challenge a premium increase)
- **An EC is required for certain compliance purposes** (see "When an EC is Still Required" below)

## Section H: Self-Certification of First Floor Height (NEW)

The 2023 edition of FEMA Form FF-206-FY-22-152 introduced **Section H: Certification of First Floor Height Based on Existing Building Records and Visual Inspection**.

This is a significant development. Section H allows a property owner or agent to **self-certify** the first floor height without a professional survey:

**How Section H Works:**
- No licensed surveyor required
- No theodolite, GPS, or RTK survey equipment needed
- Based on existing building construction documents, owner knowledge, and direct measurement with basic tools (tape measure, yardstick)
- Process:
  1. Review the original building plans (if available)
  2. Identify the lowest floor elevation from plans or construction knowledge
  3. Measure from a known reference point (e.g., foundation corner, existing grade, utility marker)
  4. Document the measurement method in Section H
  5. State your basis for the FFH determination
  6. Sign and date the certification

**Acceptable Methods for Section H:**
- Measurement from first floor to adjacent grade with tape measure
- Review of construction plans showing floor-to-grade relationships
- Comparison to similar properties with known elevations
- Measurement from first floor to utility marker (e.g., gas meter, electric box at grade level)
- Documentation from community records or prior professionals

**Limitations of Section H:**
- No conversion to BFE; the elevation is reported relative to the referenced datum or ground
- Less authoritative than a professional survey (some lenders may not accept it)
- If there is dispute about accuracy, a professional EC is the standard resolution
- For complex situations (basements, crawlspaces with variable grade, substantial enclosures), a professional survey is still preferred

**When Section H is Appropriate:**
- Single-story single-family homes on slab foundations
- Properties with uncomplicated grade and clear floor definition
- When the goal is to document the building's characteristics for Increased Cost of Compliance or premium adjustment
- When the owner has reliable existing documentation (construction plans, prior surveys)

**When Section H is Insufficient:**
- Basement or crawlspace properties (requires professional elevation measurement)
- Multi-story buildings with multiple floor elevations
- Properties with steep or variable grade
- LOMC applications (requires professional certification)
- New construction or substantial improvement floodplain compliance (usually requires professional EC)
- Properties in dispute regarding elevation

## The Fillable EC Tool: agents.floodsmart.gov/fill-ec

The FEMA/NFIP web platform now provides a fillable Elevation Certificate form at agents.floodsmart.gov/fill-ec. This tool:

- Hosts the current FEMA form (FF-206-FY-22-152, 2023 Edition)
- Allows professionals to fill sections A-I using a web interface
- Saves drafts in a FEMA database (temporary storage for 24 hours)
- Generates a PDF for printing and signing
- Provides some built-in validation (checks for missing required fields)
- Links to mapping tools to verify FIRM panel information
- Allows users to research BFE and other flood hazard data

**Using the Tool:**
1. Navigate to agents.floodsmart.gov/fill-ec
2. Log in with FloodSmart credentials (free account required)
3. Enter property address or parcel number
4. The system auto-populates BFE, FIRM panel, and community information
5. Complete Sections A-I manually
6. Download the PDF and print for professional signature
7. Scan and submit to the insurer or lender

**Important Notes:**
- The online form is convenient but not required (paper forms are still valid)
- The form auto-populated data (BFE, FIRM) should be verified against current official sources
- Some fields require manual entry (measurements, professional opinions)
- The tool does not validate the accuracy of your measurements—you must ensure survey accuracy

## Community Rating System (CRS) and Elevation Certificates

The Community Rating System is a NFIP program that recognizes and rewards communities that exceed minimum floodplain management standards. Participating communities receive a discount on insurance premiums for all policyholders in that community.

**How CRS Relates to ECs:**
- Communities earn CRS points by completing flood mitigation activities (elevation, acquisition, mitigation planning, etc.)
- One of the point-earning activities is promoting ECs: communities that encourage property owners to obtain ECs earn credits
- Example: If a community adopts a local ordinance requiring ECs for new construction or substantial improvements, the community can earn CRS credit
- Result: The community moves up in CRS rating (e.g., from Class 9 to Class 8), and all residents receive a 5% premium discount (Class 8) or higher

**Premium Impact:**
- CRS Class 1 (highest): 45% community-wide discount
- CRS Class 5: 20% community-wide discount
- CRS Class 9 (lowest): 5% community-wide discount
- No CRS rating: 0% discount

**Individual Property Impact:**
- If a property owner provides an EC to the community (in communities with CRS programs), the property may be flagged as compliant and may receive priority consideration for CRS benefits
- The discount is applied community-wide, not property-specific, but properties that help the community achieve better CRS ratings indirectly benefit everyone

## When an EC is Still REQUIRED (Not Optional)

Despite being optional for Risk Rating 2.0 insurance rating, ECs remain **mandatory** in these situations:

### 1. Floodplain Management Compliance for New Construction
- When a building is newly constructed in a Special Flood Hazard Area, local floodplain management ordinances require an EC certifying that the lowest floor is at or above BFE
- This is enforced by the local building/floodplain management department, not the insurance company
- The EC must be submitted before the certificate of occupancy is issued
- Failure to provide an EC prevents occupancy; the building cannot be legally inhabited

### 2. Substantial Improvement Compliance
- When a building undergoes substantial improvement (renovations costing 50% or more of assessed value), NFIP regulations require an EC certifying new lowest floor elevation post-improvement
- This applies even if the building already had a prior EC
- Purpose: to ensure the improvement project met elevation requirements

### 3. LOMC (Letter of Map Change) Applications
- When applying for a LOMA, LOMR-F, or conditional version of either, the EC with certified LAG (Lowest Adjacent Grade) elevation is a required supporting document
- See File 08 (LOMC Process) for detailed requirements

### 4. Lender or Investor Requirements
- Some lenders, particularly those following guidelines from Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac, require ECs for all properties in Special Flood Hazard Areas
- Some investors in NFIP policies require ECs for portfolio properties to reduce rating uncertainty
- These are contractual requirements, not NFIP rules, but they effectively make ECs required for the property owner

### 5. Increased Cost of Compliance (ICC) Coverage
- When a property owner claims ICC coverage (coverage for the cost to bring a flood-damaged building into compliance with new elevation standards), the NFIP may require an EC documenting the pre-flood first floor elevation to calculate the compliance cost

## The Strategic Value of an EC in Risk Rating 2.0

Given the optional nature of ECs, property owners should evaluate whether obtaining one is cost-effective:

**Benefits of Obtaining an EC:**
- Potential premium reduction if FFH is higher than FEMA's estimate (often the case)
- Certainty and documentation of baseline elevation (useful for future claims, sales, renovations)
- Faster underwriting if you provide the EC upfront (no delays waiting for FEMA data)
- Protection against future premium increases due to dataset "corrections" (your EC freezes the FFH)
- Compliance with LOMC or new construction requirements

**Costs of Obtaining an EC:**
- Professional survey: $400-$800 for a standard residential EC (varies by region and complexity)
- Time: typically 2-4 weeks from order to delivery
- Time for submission and processing: 1-2 weeks for NFIP to update the policy

**Break-Even Analysis:**
- If the EC shows 4+ feet of FFH elevation advantage over FEMA's estimate, annual savings exceed $300-$500
- Break-even period: typically 1-2 years
- Mortgage-term value: 15-year mortgage, potential savings of $5,000-$10,000

**Recommendation:**
- For properties in SFHAs (high-risk zones): obtaining an EC is highly recommended—the probability of FEMA underestimating elevation is high, and savings typically exceed costs within 2 years
- For properties in Zone X (moderate to low risk): the cost-benefit is weaker, but an EC is still valuable for documentation
- For all properties: an EC is prudent before selling or refinancing in a high-risk zone

## Data Quality and Documentation

When submitting an EC to FEMA for premium adjustment, understand that:

1. **FEMA will verify your EC data** against its own sources (flood maps, LiDAR, historical data)
2. **Extreme outliers may be questioned** (e.g., if your EC shows FFH = 12 feet and LiDAR showed 4 feet, FEMA may request additional documentation)
3. **Section D comments are critical** for justifying unusual values or explaining data sources
4. **Professional credentials matter**: an EC signed by a PE or certified surveyor carries more weight than a Section H self-certification
5. **Photographs are evidence**: high-quality photos showing the foundation, grade reference points, and measurement basis support your numbers

ECs are now financial documents. Accuracy and documentation are no longer just professional best practices—they are business necessities.

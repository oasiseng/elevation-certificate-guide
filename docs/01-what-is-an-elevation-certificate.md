# What Is an Elevation Certificate?

## Definition and Purpose

An Elevation Certificate (EC) is an administrative documentation tool of the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) that certifies the elevations of a building in relation to the base flood elevation (BFE) in effect for that property's location. It is not a survey—though surveys may be used to develop the elevations it documents. Rather, it is a standardized FEMA form (currently Form FF-206-FY-22-152, 2023 Edition) that captures measured building and property information in a format that insurance companies, local floodplain managers, and property owners can rely on for consistent flood risk assessment and compliance purposes.

The document serves as the definitive record of a building's lowest floor elevation and associated structural information, replacing estimates or assumptions with verified measurements. For properties in or near Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs), this precision can fundamentally change flood insurance costs and regulatory obligations.

---

## The Three Primary Uses

### 1. NFIP Flood Insurance Rating
The most common reason property owners obtain an Elevation Certificate is to establish their actual First Floor Height (FFH) for insurance premium calculation. Under the NFIP, a property's flood insurance premium depends substantially on the relationship between its lowest occupiable floor and the base flood elevation for its area.

The traditional rating system, Zone-based rating, assessed premiums primarily by flood zone. An AE zone property might pay significantly more simply because of its location, regardless of whether the building actually sat well above the BFE. A properly completed EC that documents a First Floor Height above the BFE can substantially reduce premiums because it demonstrates lower actual flood risk.

**Premium impact example** (for a $250,000 property in AE zone):
- Building 4 feet below BFE: approximately $9,500 per year
- Building at BFE: approximately $1,410 per year
- Building 3 feet above BFE: approximately $427 per year

This difference illustrates why the EC is often the highest-return investment a property owner in a flood zone can make. In many cases, the cost of an EC ($1,500–$5,000 depending on building complexity and professional required) pays for itself within a single year through premium reduction.

### 2. Floodplain Management and Code Compliance
Local floodplain managers use Elevation Certificates during permit review and code enforcement to verify that proposed or existing construction complies with community flood ordinances. Most communities adopt the FEMA flood insurance study and floodplain requirements as part of their local code. When a property owner applies for construction permits, renovation permits, or substantial damage repair permits, the community often requires an EC to confirm that the work meets minimum elevation requirements.

An EC also provides the factual foundation for administrative appeals or variances. If a property owner believes their home's actual elevation differs from the mapped BFE, they may submit an EC as evidence supporting a request for a Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA) or community variance.

### 3. Letters of Map Change and Regulatory Relief
Property owners in areas later determined to be outside the SFHA, or in areas where improved elevation data contradicts the official FIRM (Flood Insurance Rate Map), can request a Letter of Map Change (LOMC). A Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA) is requested when property improvements or new elevation data indicate a property is outside the SFHA. A Letter of Map Revision based on Fill (LOMR-F) addresses properties where fill has been placed.

An Elevation Certificate, particularly one completed by a licensed surveyor or engineer, provides the technical foundation FEMA requires to process these requests. In fact, for many LOMA submissions, an EC is mandatory. The EC transforms an informal claim ("I think my house is higher than the flood map shows") into a professionally verified assertion that FEMA and the community can evaluate objectively.

## History and Evolution of the Form

The Elevation Certificate as a concept emerged in the early years of the NFIP as communities and insurers recognized the need for consistent elevation documentation. The form has evolved through multiple editions as the program matured, community requirements changed, and risk assessment methodology advanced.

The current form, FEMA Form FF-206-FY-22-152 (2023 Edition), reflects decades of practical experience. Previous editions required property owners and professionals to navigate ambiguous terminology and incomplete guidance. The 2023 Edition standardizes language, clarifies which sections require professional certification, and explicitly addresses modern concerns like crawlspace documentation, enclosure details, and flood opening requirements.

Understanding that the form is the **2023 Edition** (OMB Control No. 1660-0008, expiring 06/30/2026) is critical. Older editions—2013, 2017, and earlier—remain in circulation, and some communities or insurers may still accept them. However, current best practice and FEMA guidance center on the 2023 Edition. If you are preparing a new EC, use the 2023 Edition.

## Why Elevation Certificates Matter: The Financial and Regulatory Reality

For a property owner, the EC often represents the single most cost-effective mitigation action available. Unlike elevating the structure (which costs $100,000+), acquiring flood insurance buyouts, or accepting high premiums indefinitely, a properly completed EC costs a fraction of annual premiums and produces immediate savings.

The EC matters equally for regulatory compliance. Communities that participate in the NFIP must enforce minimum elevation requirements. A property owner who undertakes renovation, reconstruction, or substantial improvements is obligated to meet the community's flood-resistant construction standards. An EC is the primary mechanism both the property owner and the community use to document compliance. Without it, the community cannot issue a final permit, and the property owner has no objective record of meeting the standard.

For insurance purposes, the EC is the definitive statement of First Floor Height. When a claim is filed following a flood, the insurer will reference the EC (if one exists) to determine if the loss is eligible for coverage or if a deductible adjustment applies. An outdated, incorrect, or missing EC can result in disputed claims or uninsured losses.

## Key Characteristics: What Doesn't Expire and What Does

A completed and submitted Elevation Certificate, once it enters the property's record at the local floodplain management office or with the insurance company, **does not expire unless there is a physical change to the building itself.**

This is a common misconception. Many property owners and agents believe ECs must be renewed every few years. In reality, an EC remains valid indefinitely if:

- The building's lowest floor elevation has not changed (Sections A8/A9)
- The structure has not been raised or modified (Section C elevations)
- No fill or grading has been added (Section E)
- No enclosures, crawlspaces, or flood openings have been altered (Section H)

The building's owner can change—the EC remains in the public record. The base flood elevation can change due to new flood studies or FIRM updates—the EC's certification of the building's actual elevation does not become invalid, though its relationship to the (new) BFE may change.

What **does** require a new EC is a structural modification. If a building is elevated, if a crawlspace is added or altered, if flood vents are removed or resized, if the building is moved or reconstructed, a new EC should be prepared to document the change. The local floodplain manager and insurance company rely on the most recent EC, and submitting outdated information following a modification can complicate claims or compliance verification.

## The Shift: Risk Rating 2.0 and the Changing Role of Elevation Certificates

Beginning in 2021, the NFIP introduced Risk Rating 2.0, a fundamental departure from the zone-based rating system that had dominated for decades. Under Risk Rating 2.0, flood insurance premiums are calculated not primarily by flood zone, but by property-specific factors including:

- Proximity to flood source (distance to the nearest river, coast, or high-risk area)
- Property-specific elevation relative to flood sources
- Occupancy type (single-family residential, commercial, multi-unit)
- Construction type and age
- Number of floors
- Foundation type
- Cost to rebuild
- Historical claims

This change has profound implications for Elevation Certificates. Under the zone-based system, an EC in an AE zone almost always resulted in substantial premium savings by documenting above-BFE elevation. Under Risk Rating 2.0, the benefit varies more widely—a property far from the flood source in an AE zone might see modest savings, while a property very close to the source but well-elevated might see dramatic savings.

Importantly, **Elevation Certificates are now optional under Risk Rating 2.0 for rating purposes**. Insurers are not required to ask for or accept them. However, many still do, and for property owners seeking to lower premiums, submitting a professionally prepared EC that documents accurate First Floor Height remains a prudent step. The EC continues to serve critical purposes beyond insurance rating: floodplain compliance, LOMA support, and the property's permanent administrative record.

## What an Elevation Certificate Is Not

An Elevation Certificate is not:

- **A survey** — While a licensed surveyor may prepare one, engineers and architects may also complete certain sections. The EC is a NFIP administrative form, not a boundary survey or topographic survey.
- **A guarantee of coverage** — An EC documenting above-BFE elevation does not guarantee flood insurance will cover any particular loss. Coverage depends on the policy terms, exclusions, and the nature of the loss.
- **A building permit or approval** — An EC documents elevations but does not constitute approval for construction. Local permit authorities review ECs as part of permitting but make their own determinations about compliance.
- **A title document** — An EC is not recorded as a deed, lien, or title instrument. It is recorded in the floodplain management office or insurance file as an administrative record.
- **A permanent solution to flooding** — An EC documents elevation above BFE but does not address drainage, groundwater, or other flooding sources (pluvial, groundwater, poor drainage) that may not be related to the mapped special flood hazard area.

Understanding these distinctions prevents misaligned expectations and helps property owners, professionals, and managers use the EC for its intended purpose.

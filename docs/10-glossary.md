# Elevation Certificate Glossary

This glossary defines all key terms related to FEMA Elevation Certificates and flood insurance.

## A

**Advisory Base Flood Elevation (ABFE)**
Updated flood hazard data developed after a disaster and released before the official Flood Insurance Study revision. Provides interim guidance on estimated flood risk.

## B

**Base Flood**
The flood elevation having a 1% chance of being equaled or exceeded in any given year. Commonly called the "100-year flood" (though the 1% annual chance means it could occur in consecutive years).

**Base Flood Elevation (BFE)**
The water surface elevation of the base flood that has been adopted by a community and shown on the Flood Insurance Rate Map. Expressed in feet above a specified vertical datum (NAVD 1988 or NGVD 1929).

**Breakaway Walls**
Non-supporting walls in V Zones (coastal high-hazard areas) designed to collapse under flood loads without damaging the main structure. Must meet specific engineering standards to allow the building itself to survive wave action and debris impact.

## C

**CBRS**
Coastal Barrier Resources System — federal designation protecting undeveloped coastal areas from certain federal development investments and subsidies, including flood insurance availability restrictions.

**Community Rating System (CRS)**
A NFIP incentive program where communities exceeding minimum floodplain management standards earn premium discounts for their flood insurance policyholders (up to 45% reduction).

**Crawlspace**
An enclosed space (typically less than 4 feet high) beneath a building above grade level, bounded by walls. Must meet specific standards for enclosure and flood venting.

## E

**Elevation Certificate (EC)**
The FEMA standard administrative form (FEMA Form 086-0-33) documenting the elevation of a building and other flood-related information. Used by insurance companies for rating and by communities for floodplain management compliance.

**Enclosure**
An area below an elevated building limited to parking, access, or storage. Must use open foundation design (piers, pilings, columns) or meet flood venting requirements. Cannot be used as living space.

## F

**FEMA**
Federal Emergency Management Agency — the agency responsible for the National Flood Insurance Program and flood hazard mapping.

**FIRM**
Flood Insurance Rate Map — the official map showing flood hazard zones, Base Flood Elevations, and flood-prone areas for a community. Updated periodically as new data becomes available.

**FIS**
Flood Insurance Study — detailed technical report supporting the Flood Insurance Rate Map, containing flood elevation data, floodway information, and methodology.

**First Floor Height (FFH)**
The elevation of the building's lowest floor measured from the adjacent grade. Used under Risk Rating 2.0 methodology. Can be self-certified by property owners under Section H of the Elevation Certificate.

**Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM)**
See FIRM (above).

**Floodplain**
The land area susceptible to the base flood, generally coinciding with the Special Flood Hazard Area shown on the FIRM.

**Floodproofing**
Making a building watertight below the Base Flood Elevation through dry floodproofing (sealing walls and openings) or wet floodproofing (allowing flood waters to enter). For non-residential buildings or residential basements under specific FEMA exception criteria only.

**Floodway**
The channel of a river and the adjacent floodplain area that must remain free of obstruction to carry floodwaters without increasing the base flood elevation. Development restrictions are stricter in floodways than in the broader SFHA.

**Flood Opening**
A permanent opening (window, door, vent, or grate) in foundation walls below the Base Flood Elevation that allows floodwaters to enter and equalize pressure, reducing structural damage. Must meet FEMA standards for size and placement.

## H

**Highest Adjacent Grade (HAG)**
The highest point of ground elevation immediately adjacent to the building. Used for reference when measuring First Floor Height and other elevations.

## L

**Lowest Adjacent Grade (LAG)**
The lowest point of ground elevation immediately adjacent to the building footprint. Used as the reference point for measuring First Floor Height.

**Letter of Map Amendment (LOMA)**
A FEMA determination that a property is not subject to the base flood, often based on an elevation certificate showing the lowest floor above the BFE. Removes the property from SFHA.

**Letter of Map Revision Based on Fill (LOMR-F)**
A FEMA map revision removing one or more properties from the flood hazard area based on fill placement. Used when the community fills an area and raises ground elevation above the BFE.

**Conditional LOMA (CLOMA)**
A conditional determination issued when data is incomplete or contingent on completion of protective measures.

**Conditional LOMR-F (CLOMR-F)**
A conditional map revision based on proposed fill, pending project completion.

**LiMWA**
Limit of Moderate Wave Action — the inland boundary of the coastal zone where wave heights during the base flood reach 1 to 3 feet. Important for determining V Zone (high-velocity) vs AE Zone boundaries in coastal areas.

**Lowest Floor**
The lowest finished floor of a building, including basements if present. Used for flood insurance rating purposes. An Elevation Certificate documents this elevation.

## M

**Machinery and Equipment (M&E)**
Building equipment including furnace, water heater, heat pump, air conditioning, and elevator (if present). Must be elevated above the Base Flood Elevation or protected by floodproofing. Elevation of the lowest M&E item affects insurance rating.

## N

**NAVD 1988**
North American Vertical Datum of 1988 — the current standard vertical reference system used on modern FIRMs and in most contemporary surveys. Replaced NGVD 1929.

**NFIP**
National Flood Insurance Program — the federally-backed flood insurance program administered through private insurers, providing flood coverage unavailable in the private market.

**NGVD 1929**
National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929 — the vertical reference system used on older FIRMs. Differs from NAVD 1988 by varying amounts depending on location (typically 1-3 feet). Elevation Certificates must convert between datums when necessary.

## O

**Otherwise Protected Area (OPA)**
An area mapped in zone X (minimal flood risk) because it is protected by a levee, floodwall, or similar structure meeting FEMA standards. Subject to residual risk.

## P

**Post-FIRM**
A building constructed after the community's first FIRM adoption date or after December 31, 1974 (whichever is later). Post-FIRM buildings must meet current building and floodplain management standards.

**Pre-FIRM**
A building constructed before the community's first FIRM adoption date. Pre-FIRM buildings are grandfathered from compliance with current standards but may be elevated or retrofitted for insurance premium savings.

**Preliminary Map**
An updated Flood Insurance Rate Map released for public review and comment before official adoption by FEMA and the community.

## R

**Risk Rating 2.0**
FEMA's current insurance rating methodology that uses property-specific factors including First Floor Height, exposure to flood hazards, and location rather than zone-based ratings. Replaced the older rating by flood zone and obstruction alone.

## S

**Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA)**
The high-risk flood zone identified on the FIRM where flood insurance is mandatory for federally-backed mortgages. Includes zones beginning with "A" or "V" and other designated high-risk areas.

**Substantial Improvement**
A property improvement (repair, reconstruction, addition) where the cost equals or exceeds 50% of the market value of the building before improvement. Triggers compliance with current floodplain management standards, including elevation requirements.

## T

**Typical Horizontal Accuracy**
The expected positional accuracy of features mapped on a FIRM, typically 1/100th of an inch at map scale (often ±50-200 feet depending on the map scale and data source).

## V

**Variance**
A community-issued exception to floodplain development standards, typically allowing development below the BFE. Requires demonstrated hardship and an Elevation Certificate documenting compliance with approved elevation.

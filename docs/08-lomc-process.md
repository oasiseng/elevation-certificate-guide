# Letters of Map Change: EC Requirements and Process

## Overview of LOMC Types

A Letter of Map Change (LOMC) is a FEMA determination that modifies a property's flood risk designation on the official Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM). There are four types of LOMCs, each involving elevation data and potentially requiring an Elevation Certificate.

## LOMA: Letter of Map Amendment

A LOMA removes a property from the Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) based on a determination that the property's natural elevation is above the Base Flood Elevation.

### When LOMA Applies:
- Property is currently mapped in a flood zone (AE, A, VE, AR, A99, etc.)
- Owner believes the property is actually at a higher elevation than the mapped BFE
- No construction, fill placement, or structural changes are being proposed
- Applicant is relying on natural grade elevation to support the LOMA

### EC Requirements for LOMA:
- **LAG (Lowest Adjacent Grade) must be certified** by a licensed professional (Professional Engineer, Land Surveyor, or Architect)
- The EC must provide the LAG elevation for the subject property
- LAG is measured to: the natural ground surface immediately adjacent to the structure
- The EC must also provide the BFE value used for comparison
- The professional must certify that the LAG exceeds the applicable BFE

### Section D Critical Notes for LOMA:
- State the purpose: "LOMA application"
- Confirm the professional's license type and state
- Note the data source for the BFE being used (FIRM panel, community floodplain map, hydro study)
- Document any assumptions about grade changes or site conditions
- Example: "Licensed Professional Engineer NY #12345. Property LAG = 12.8 feet NAVD 1988. Applicable BFE from FIRM panel 36001C0345G dated 04/17/2007 = 10.5 feet. LAG exceeds BFE; property should be removed from SFHA."

### LOMA Success Requirements:
- LAG elevation must be documented by a professional with survey-grade accuracy
- LAG must exceed the BFE by typically at least 0.5-1.0 feet (communities vary; check local requirements)
- No fill or construction may have artificially raised the elevation (or the fill must be documented separately)
- The property must not have been flood-damaged in a way that would contradict the elevation data

### What LOMA Removes:
- Federal flood insurance mandate (if property is federally backed, flood insurance is no longer required)
- Requirement to maintain flood insurance during mortgage term
- FEMA designation as a floodplain property

### What LOMA Does NOT Remove:
- Lender's independent requirement for flood insurance (lender can still require it)
- Community floodplain management oversight (if the community has restrictions, they apply regardless of FEMA mapping)
- State or local flood insurance requirements

### LOMA Timeline:
- Typical processing: 2-4 weeks for simple cases; 6-8 weeks for complex cases
- FEMA provides a preliminary determination within 2 weeks (formal letter issued later)
- Revised FIRM is issued (updated Flood Insurance Rate Map panel)

## CLOMA: Conditional Letter of Map Amendment

A CLOMA is a preliminary LOMA issued before a project is built or before existing conditions change. It is a conditional determination that confirms FEMA's intention to issue a full LOMA if the conditions stated in the CLOMA are met.

### When CLOMA Applies:
- A property is under construction and will be elevated above the current BFE
- Owner wants preliminary confirmation that the new elevation will qualify for LOMA before building is complete
- Developer building a new subdivision above the BFE wants advance assurance before construction begins

### EC Requirements for CLOMA:
- An EC showing **proposed** LAG elevation is typically required
- The EC is based on construction plans and specifications
- The professional certifies the elevation that will result after construction
- The EC is marked "Proposed" or "As Designed" in Section D
- Example: "This certificate documents the proposed lowest adjacent grade for the subject property following construction of the residence per plans dated 05/15/2023. Upon construction completion, the natural grade will be 13.2 feet NAVD 1988, exceeding the applicable BFE of 10.5 feet."

### CLOMA Conditions and Conversion to LOMA:
- The CLOMA is issued conditioned on: "upon completion of construction as designed"
- Once construction is complete, the owner requests conversion of the CLOMA to a final LOMA
- A follow-up EC documenting the as-built LAG is required to confirm the conditions were met
- If the as-built elevation differs materially from the proposed elevation, FEMA may deny the final LOMA

### CLOMA Timeline:
- Issued within 4 weeks of application
- Valid for 2 years (renewable)
- Conversion to LOMA occurs after construction completion and final EC submission

## LOMR-F: Letter of Map Revision Based on Fill

A LOMR-F modifies the mapped flood hazard based on fill placement. When fill is added to a property or community, the land surface rises, potentially placing the property above the BFE.

### When LOMR-F Applies:
- Fill has been placed on the property, raising the grade above the BFE
- Fill was placed by the property owner or developer (typically for foundation construction, site elevation, or compliance with other regulations)
- Property owner wants FEMA to revise the mapping to reflect the new higher elevation

### Fill vs. Natural Grade:
- **Natural Grade (LOMA):** the original undisturbed ground elevation
- **Fill (LOMR-F):** added material (soil, sand, compacted material) that raises the surface
- If a property's elevation is achieved by fill, LOMR-F is used; if by natural elevation, LOMA is used
- Important: fill can be washed away or displaced, so LOMR-F sometimes receives closer scrutiny than LOMA

### EC Requirements for LOMR-F:
- An EC showing the **fill elevation** (also called "finished grade" or "construction fill elevation") is required
- The professional must certify:
  - The elevation of the fill surface (top of fill)
  - The extent of the fill (area covered)
  - The elevation at which the fill was placed
  - That the fill was placed in compliance with local regulations
- The EC must also show the pre-fill elevation (natural grade before fill) for comparison
- Example Section D entry: "Professional survey conducted 06/20/2023. Natural grade (pre-fill) at subject property = 8.2 feet NAVD 1988. Fill elevation (top of fill) = 12.5 feet NAVD 1988. Fill material: compacted engineered fill placed per community standards. Property elevation now exceeds BFE of 10.5 feet."

### Fill Elevation Documentation:
- The EC should include reference points where fill begins and ends
- Documentation of fill material and compaction (if available from contractor)
- Certification that the fill is permanent and stable (not temporary)
- Note whether fill is within the property boundary or extends beyond

### LOMR-F vs. New Hazard:
- FEMA evaluates whether the fill placement creates a new flood hazard upstream or downstream
- If the fill placement redirects floodwater and damages adjacent properties, FEMA may deny the LOMR-F or impose conditions
- This is why fill-based requests sometimes take longer and may require a hydrologic study

### LOMR-F Risks:
- If fill is removed or displaced, the property reverts to original flood hazard
- Property with LOMR-F may have difficulty with future insurance if fill is compromised
- Community may restrict or limit fill placement in flood-prone areas

### LOMR-F Timeline:
- Typical processing: 4-8 weeks
- May require hydraulic study: 8-12 weeks or longer

## CLOMR-F: Conditional Letter of Map Revision Based on Fill

A CLOMR-F is a conditional LOMR-F issued before fill is actually placed. It confirms that FEMA will issue a final LOMR-F if fill is placed as described.

### When CLOMR-F Applies:
- Developer is planning site elevation via fill placement
- Applicant wants conditional approval before construction begins
- Used in subdivision development to confirm that future homesites will be mapped out of SFHA after fill placement

### EC Requirements for CLOMR-F:
- An EC showing the **proposed** fill elevation is required
- Based on site plans and grading plans
- Professional certifies: "Upon placement of fill to elevation 12.5 feet NAVD 1988 as shown on grading plan dated 05/01/2023, the subject property will exceed the applicable BFE."
- The EC is conditional and marked as "Proposed" or "As Designed"

### Conversion to Final LOMR-F:
- After fill is placed, owner submits a final EC documenting as-built fill elevation
- FEMA compares as-built to proposed; if they match (within acceptable tolerance), final LOMR-F is issued
- Tolerance is typically ±0.5 feet

## [FEMA MT-1 Technical Guidance (Doc 65, Nov 2019)](https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_guidance_mt-1_technical_guidance_nov_2024.pdf): LAG and Fill Modifications for Existing Structures

FEMA's MT-1 technical guidance (Document 65, Section 4.13, p.43) draws a clear distinction between **legitimate site grading** and **retrofit attempts to manipulate LAG** for an existing building.

### Core Principle

- Fill used to raise land above BFE is intended to occur **before** building construction.
- For existing structures, placing fill around a building to increase reported LAG is generally **not recommended** and may violate NFIP requirements.
- If the objective is flood protection through structural or site modification, FEMA typically expects an **MT-2 pathway** (LOMR/CLOMR), not a standard MT-1-only approach.

### Retaining Wall Decision Branch (Critical for LAG)

When retaining walls are present, FEMA's LAG interpretation depends on whether the building relies on the wall:

1. **Building is set back from retaining wall (not structurally dependent):**
   - MT-1 review is typically allowed.
   - LAG is generally taken at/adjacent to the building foundation.
   - FEMA may still perform additional case review and override measurement location if warranted by design conditions.

2. **Building is supported by retaining wall (structural dependence exists):**
   - LAG is taken at the **lowest point where grade meets the retaining wall**.
   - This configuration is commonly associated with a likely non-removal outcome.

### Existing Structure Retrofit Attempts to Increase LAG

Potentially acceptable only on a strict, case-by-case basis:
- Fill is properly compacted.
- Fill is appropriately sloped and stable.
- Work creates a true adjacent grade condition (not merely engineered enclosure).

Typically not acceptable:
- "Sandwich" fill retained on all sides by walls with no true natural-ground interface.
- Curbs or similar perimeter additions intended only to raise grade readings at foundation edges.
- Any configuration that does not represent genuine adjacent grade.

### Required Sequence Before Any LAG-Altering Work

1. Consult the local floodplain administrator.
2. Confirm local/state rules and any standards stricter than FEMA minimum criteria.
3. Submit **CLOMR-F before construction/fill placement** when proposing fill-based revision strategy.

This sequencing is essential to avoid expensive non-compliant work that FEMA may later reject.

### Practical Screening Logic (Engineering/Review Use)

Use this as a high-level triage framework for project scoping:

```text
IF building_exists:
    IF fill_added_to_change_LAG:
        REJECT (likely NFIP violation)
    IF modification_for_flood_protection:
        REQUIRE MT-2 (LOMR/CLOMR)

IF retaining_wall_present:
    IF building_setback_from_wall:
        LAG = foundation_elevation
    ELSE IF building_supported_by_wall:
        LAG = lowest_grade_at_wall
        RESULT = likely_non_removal

IF retrofit_attempt:
    IF fill_is_compacted AND sloped AND creates_true_grade:
        POSSIBLE (case-by-case)
    ELSE:
        REJECT

BEFORE_any_modification:
    REQUIRE local_floodplain_approval
    REQUIRE CLOMR-F submission
```

### Implementation Takeaways

- LAG must represent **true adjacent ground**, not engineered workarounds.
- Existing structures are heavily constrained for LAG modification claims.
- Retaining wall geometry and structural dependence are often dispositive.
- Pre-approval and documentation discipline (especially CLOMR-F and local review) should be treated as mandatory gate checks.

## Elevation Certificate Requirements for All LOMC Types

### LAG and HAG Definitions (Critical Distinction)

**LAG (Lowest Adjacent Grade):** The natural ground surface immediately adjacent to the building. Used for LOMA and LOMR-F to determine if the property is above BFE.

**HAG (Highest Adjacent Grade):** For buildings on pilings or elevated, HAG is the highest natural ground elevation within a specified distance (typically 12 feet) of the building. Used to evaluate if the structure is properly elevated.

For LOMC purposes:
- LOMA applicants provide LAG (for natural elevation determinations)
- LOMR-F applicants provide fill surface elevation (not LAG)
- LOMC applicants should NOT confuse LAG with building floor elevation; LOMC is about land, not the building

### Professional Credentials for LOMC ECs

FEMA requires that LAG elevations for LOMC applications be certified by one of the following:

1. **Licensed Professional Engineer (PE)** in the state where the property is located
2. **Licensed Land Surveyor (LS)** in the state where the property is located
3. **Licensed Architect (AIA)** with surveying experience (less common; PE or LS preferred)
4. **Military or Government Professional** (for federal properties)

The professional must include:
- License number
- License state
- Professional stamp or seal
- Signature and date

**No EC for LOMC can be signed by a technician, draftsperson, or unlicensed person**, even if supervised. FEMA requires the named professional to take personal responsibility.

### Datum and Conversion for LOMC

The LAG elevation must be documented in a **specific datum**, typically:
- **NAVD 1988 (North American Vertical Datum of 1988):** current standard, preferred by FEMA
- **NGVD 1929 (National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929):** older datum, some communities still use it

**Critical Rule:** The LAG elevation must be in the same datum as the BFE being compared against. If the BFE is in NGVD 1929 (from an older FIRM), the LAG must be converted to NGVD 1929 or the BFE must be converted to NAVD 1988.

Conversion Factors:
- NGVD 1929 to NAVD 1988: varies by location but typically +0.4 to +2.5 feet
- **Never guess the conversion factor.** Use NOAA VERTCON online tool or consult the community/FEMA

**Section D must document:** "Elevation provided in NAVD 1988. BFE from FIRM panel 36001C0345G (dated 04/17/2007) is in NGVD 1929 = 10.5 ft. NAVD 1988 equivalent = 12.1 ft (conversion factor 1.6 feet from VERTCON). Property LAG = 12.8 feet NAVD 1988, exceeds converted BFE."

### Supporting Documentation for LOMC ECs

Beyond the EC itself, the professional should provide:

1. **Survey Notes:** field notes documenting the LAG measurement method, equipment used, grade verification
2. **Photographs:** 4+ high-quality photos showing the foundation/grade area, measurement references, site context
3. **Calculation Sheet:** documenting how LAG was derived from field measurements
4. **Reference Points:** identification of nearby survey monuments, benchmarks, or established elevation points
5. **Datum Documentation:** source of the datum (survey monument, GPS calibration, etc.)

### BFE Determination for LOMC

The applicant must determine the correct BFE to compare against the LAG:

1. **Review the Current FIRM Panel** for the subject property
   - Identify the panel number and effective date
   - Locate the property's zone and read the BFE from the panel legend
   - Document the FIRM panel number and date on the EC

2. **If No BFE is Shown on the FIRM** (e.g., property is in Zone A but no contour lines are provided):
   - **Contact the Community Floodplain Manager** for the official BFE
   - The community may have a more detailed floodplain map or hydro study with BFE information
   - Document the source: "BFE obtained from [Community Name] Floodplain Manager [Name], dated [Date]"

3. **If No BFE is Available from Community Sources:**
   - Applicant can request FEMA Map Assistance Center help: 1-877-FEMA-MAP or visit the FEMA Map Service Center
   - A qualified engineer may need to develop a BFE via hydro study (expensive and time-consuming; reserved for special circumstances)

4. **Document Everything:**
   - EC Section D: "BFE = 10.5 feet NGVD 1929 from FIRM panel 36001C0345G, dated 04/17/2007"
   - Always include the FIRM panel number and effective date

## Submitting the LOMC Application

### MT-EZ Application (Simple Properties)
- Used for properties with simple elevation profiles (single structure, no complex grades)
- Typically for single-family homes and small commercial buildings
- One EC is required (showing LAG or fill elevation)

### MT-1 Application (Complex Properties)
- Used for properties with complex features (multiple structures, variable grades, hydro concerns)
- May require multiple ECs (one per structure or per flood zone)
- May require additional hydro study if fill placement affects flood conveyance

### Submission Process
1. Complete the LOMC application (MT-EZ or MT-1)
2. Attach the EC (signed by licensed professional with seal/stamp)
3. Attach supporting documentation (survey notes, photos, calculations)
4. Submit to: FEMA Headquarters, Office of Insurance, Mapping and Hazard Mitigation
   - Current address available at: FEMA.gov/floodplain/lomc
   - OR submit via the online LOMC portal (when available)
5. Pay application fee (currently $300-$500 for most applications; check FEMA website for current fee schedule)

### Timeline and Processing
- Initial completeness review: 1-2 weeks
- Technical review: 2-6 weeks
- Preliminary determination issued: 4 weeks typical
- Final determination and revised FIRM panel: issued after preliminary determination (2-4 additional weeks)
- Total: 6-12 weeks typical

## Post-LOMC: What Happens to the Insurance Requirement

### Federal Mandate Removal
- Once the LOMA or LOMR-F is approved and the FIRM is officially revised, the federal flood insurance mandate is lifted
- Federally-backed (FHA, VA, Conventional, USDA) mortgages no longer require continuous flood insurance on that property

### Lender's Independent Right
- **Important:** The property's lender retains the right to require flood insurance even after LOMA approval
- Lenders often have their own risk models and may not accept the LOMA determination
- Lender policy language typically states: "Flood insurance may be required independent of FEMA mapping"
- Applicant should verify with lender that LOMA approval will satisfy the insurance requirement

### Community Floodplain Ordinances
- If the community has floodplain development or setback restrictions, they remain in effect even after LOMA
- Removal from FEMA map does not remove local floodplain restrictions
- Example: A community may require 100-year-floodplain setbacks from the upstream direction, independent of FIRM mapping

### Future Flood Damage
- If the property is later damaged by a flood, the LOMA does not prevent an SFIP (Standard Flood Insurance Policy) from being issued
- The property can always obtain NFIP insurance, regardless of FEMA mapping
- However, removal from the SFHA typically results in lower flood insurance rates if re-enrolled

## Common LOMC Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

1. **Using the Wrong BFE**
   - Mistake: Reading BFE from an outdated FIRM panel
   - Fix: Always verify the effective date of the FIRM panel; ensure the panel is current
   - Section D: Document the specific FIRM panel number and date

2. **LAG Measured at the Wrong Location**
   - Mistake: Measuring at the lowest elevation on the property (the actual depression) rather than adjacent to the foundation
   - Fix: LAG is measured immediately adjacent to the building, not the lowest point on the lot
   - Section D: Document the measurement location: "LAG measured along west side of foundation"

3. **Confusing Datum or Not Converting**
   - Mistake: LAG in NAVD 1988, BFE in NGVD 1929, no conversion noted
   - Fix: Explicitly convert to a common datum using VERTCON or surveyor tools
   - Section D: Note the conversion factor and source

4. **Professional Seal Missing**
   - Mistake: EC signed but not sealed by licensed professional
   - Fix: Ensure PE or LS applies their professional seal/stamp to all pages
   - FEMA will reject the application if seal is missing

5. **Submitting Before Survey is Complete**
   - Mistake: Applying for LOMC before field survey has been completed
   - Fix: Do not submit until all field measurements are complete and professional has reviewed
   - Timeline: Allow 4-6 weeks for professional survey before LOMC application submission

6. **Fill Without Legal Authority**
   - Mistake: Placing fill on property without permits; LOMR-F application reveals unauthorized fill
   - Fix: Obtain local fill placement permits before submitting LOMR-F
   - Community may require fill to be removed if not properly permitted

7. **Not Documenting Reference Points**
   - Mistake: No noted benchmarks or survey monuments; elevation cannot be verified
   - Fix: Tie the survey to established USGS benchmarks or NGS survey control points
   - Attach USGS benchmark data or survey control point coordinates to EC

## Strategic Use of LOMC for Risk Rating 2.0

With Risk Rating 2.0, an approved LOMA removes a property from SFHA mapping and typically results in:
- No flood insurance mandate
- Potential for lower insurance rates through standard insurance pools
- Removal of property from FEMA flood risk assessment

However:
- A property with higher natural elevation might not need a LOMC if Risk Rating 2.0 already reflects the elevation accurately
- The EC submitted for insurance rating (showing FFH above BFE) accomplishes the premium benefit without the LOMC formal process
- LOMC is still required for new construction and substantial improvement compliance, but for existing properties, the EC + insurance submission may be the faster path to premium reduction

Summary: LOMC is a formal, time-consuming process but is legally significant for removing federal mandates and is necessary for new construction/substantial improvement compliance. For existing properties with Risk Rating 2.0, the EC submission to the insurer may achieve the financial benefit with less overhead.

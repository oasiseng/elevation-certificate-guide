# Building Diagrams Explained

## The Purpose and Importance of Building Diagrams

The FEMA Elevation Certificate includes nine building diagrams (numbered 1A, 1B, 2A, 2B, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8). Each diagram represents a distinct building structural type. Selecting the correct diagram is essential because the diagram determines how elevations are interpreted and which measurements are required.

A building categorized under the wrong diagram will produce misleading or useless elevation data. For example, if a property owner selects Diagram 1 (slab-on-grade) for a building that is actually elevated on piers (Diagram 5), the resulting "lowest floor elevation" will be meaningless—it will reflect the bottom of the pier structure rather than the actual lowest enclosed living space.

The diagrams are based on FEMA guidance documents, particularly the "As Built" Lowest Floor Guide and Technical Reference: Flood Elevation Design standards. Understanding each diagram's characteristics helps professionals and property owners select correctly.

---

## Diagram 1A and 1B: Slab-on-Grade Buildings

**What It Represents:** Buildings constructed with a concrete slab poured directly on the ground, with no basement, crawlspace, or elevation. The lowest floor is the slab itself.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the top of the slab (the floor surface on which people walk).

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** In V zones, where wave action is a hazard, the lowest floor elevation is also the top of the slab, but additional structural requirements apply (see below).

**Key Characteristics:**

- The slab sits on grade; there is no enclosed or semi-enclosed space below the living floor
- The building footprint may sit at or near finished grade (LAG)
- In A zones, this is the simplest building type for elevation purposes: measure to the slab top
- Garages, if attached, use the same slab and share the same lowest floor elevation

**V Zone Notes:** Slab-on-grade buildings in V zones are permitted but must have additional protection. The V zone requires that the space between grade and the first floor (which is zero in slab-on-grade) does not trap water. The slab must be designed to withstand wave and surge impact loads. Pilings or columns are preferred; slab-on-grade is acceptable only if the engineer confirms the slab and its foundation are designed for V zone loading.

**Diagram 1A vs. 1B:** Diagram 1A includes a garage; Diagram 1B does not. Choose 1A if the building has an attached garage; choose 1B if it does not.

**As-Built Guidance:** For slab-on-grade buildings, the "As Built" Lowest Floor elevation is straightforward: it is the top elevation of the slab.

## Diagram 2A and 2B: Buildings with Basements

**What It Represents:** Buildings with an enclosed basement or cellar space below the main living area. The basement has finished or unfinished floor space; walls may be masonry, concrete, or frame.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** In A zones, the lowest floor is the basement floor (the bottom of the basement). This reflects that the basement is an enclosed living space subject to flooding. The building is not "elevated"—it is below-grade, and the entire basement volume may fill with floodwater.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** In V zones, enclosed basements are not permitted. The lowest floor must be above the DFE (Design Flood Elevation, typically the BFE plus 1–2 feet freeboard). If a property in a V zone has a basement, that basement must be unenclosed or designed as a wet-floodable space (not containing living space, equipment, or utilities that would be damaged by saltwater intrusion). For V zone purposes, the lowest floor is the main living floor above the basement, not the basement floor itself.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Walls fully enclose the space below the main floor
- For A zones, the entire basement volume is considered exposed to flooding
- For V zones, basements are highly restricted; if present, they must not interfere with the rated lowest floor elevation

**Crawlspaces vs. Basements:** A basement is a fully enclosed space with a floor on which a person can stand and work. A crawlspace is a shallow, often partially open space beneath the building used for mechanical systems but not for living or storage (though some crawlspaces are finished). For EC purposes:

- A basement = Diagram 2 (A zones) or not permitted (V zones)
- A crawlspace = Diagram 8 (discussed below)

**Diagram 2A vs. 2B:** Diagram 2A includes an attached garage; Diagram 2B does not.

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the basement floor level. If the basement is split-level (sloped floor), document the lowest point.

## Diagram 3: Split-Level, Slab-on-Grade

**What It Represents:** Buildings constructed with multiple floor levels at different elevations, all sitting on slabs-on-grade (no basements, no crawlspaces). Common in hilly or sloped terrain.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the lowest slab, regardless of where that slab is located in the building footprint (front, back, or side).

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** Same as A zone—the elevation of the lowest slab.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Multiple finished floor levels, typically differing by 3–6 feet
- All slabs are poured on-grade; there is no below-grade or elevated space
- The lowest level is the critical measurement
- If the building has a sunken room, sunken garage, or depressed entry, those sunken elevations are documented

**Common Example:** A hillside home with a main entry at elevation 15 feet (NAVD 88) but a lower-level family room at elevation 10 feet (where the lot slopes down). The lowest floor elevation is 10 feet, not 15 feet.

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the lowest slab surface anywhere in the building.

---

## Diagram 4: Split-Level, Other Than Slab-on-Grade

**What It Represents:** Buildings with multiple floor levels where at least one of those levels is not a slab-on-grade—often a combination of basement or crawlspace at the lowest level and slab-on-grade at mid-level.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the lowest enclosed floor, which may be a basement floor, a crawlspace floor, or the lowest structural floor member if the crawlspace is only semi-enclosed.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** If a V zone building has a basement component (Diagram 4), that basement must be unenclosed or wet-floodable. The rated lowest floor is the main living floor, not the basement level.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Combines two or more building types (e.g., basement + slab-on-grade, or crawlspace + slab-on-grade)
- The lowest point in the structure may shift from area to area
- Common in older homes or homes built on sloped terrain with significant grade changes

**Example:** A house with a basement on the back side (where the lot slopes down) and a slab-on-grade living area on the front. The two levels may differ by 8–10 feet. The lowest floor elevation is the basement floor, even if the majority of the living space is higher.

**As-Built Guidance:** Identify the lowest enclosed floor level across the entire footprint and document its elevation. If the building has multiple distinct lowest-floor elevations (different basement depths in different wings), document the single lowest point.

---

## Diagram 5: Elevated on Piers, Posts, Columns, or Parallel Shear Walls (Open)

**What It Represents:** Buildings raised above grade on discrete support structures (piers, posts, columns, walls) with open space beneath (no enclosure, no lattice screening, no obstruction). The space below the building is completely open to floodwater flow.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the lowest floor or structural member of the building itself (the rim joist, first floor deck, or lowest interior floor). The ground level beneath is irrelevant; water will flow freely below the building.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** Same as A zone—the elevation of the lowest enclosed living space.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Complete vertical separation between grade and building
- Flood water flows freely beneath the structure without obstruction
- The footings and posts extend below the lowest floor but are not considered "floor"
- The building's lowest floor is clearly elevated above grade, typically by 3–6 feet or more depending on the BFE and required freeboard
- Garages, if present, may be open (carport-style) with columns, or enclosed with door openings

**Foundation Elevation:** The measurement of the posts or columns themselves is not the "lowest floor." The floor of the building (the lowest occupied or semi-occupied level) is the critical measurement. For example, if piers extend from elevation 5 feet up to elevation 12 feet, where the lowest floor sits, the lowest floor elevation is 12 feet, not 5 feet.

**Flood Opening:** Open structures inherently allow flood water to flow. No additional flood openings are required for Diagram 5 because there is no enclosure to obstruct water.

**Diagram 5 Characteristics:** Open lattice or screening that would slow water flow does not disqualify a building from Diagram 5 if the lattice is designed to break away or allow water passage. If a lattice is designed to remain intact and fully enclose the space, the building should be Diagram 6 or 7, not Diagram 5.

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the bottom elevation of the first-floor deck, rim joist, or floor system that creates the usable or occupied space.

---

## Diagram 6: Elevated on Piers, Posts, or Columns with Full or Partial Enclosure

**What It Represents:** Buildings raised above grade on piers, posts, or columns, with walls, lattice, or other obstructions enclosing some or all of the space below.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the building's lowest floor (same as Diagram 5). The enclosed space below does not count as a floor; it is an enclosure that must have flood openings.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** Same—the lowest enclosed living floor of the building itself, not the enclosure below.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Elevated structure with enclosed or partially enclosed space below
- The enclosed space may be used for storage, mechanical systems, or parking
- Walls or lattice must have flood openings to allow water to enter and exit freely
- If the enclosed space is finished or contains utilities, it must either have flood openings or be wet-floodable (designed to flood without damage)

**Flood Opening Requirements (A Zones):** The enclosed space must have openings totaling at least 1 square foot per 150 square feet of enclosed floor area. Openings must be on at least two sides to allow water circulation. Openings must include relief of interior hydrostatic pressure (typically holes in partition walls or gates/screens in door openings that remain open or break away when water rises). In V zones, breakaway walls or openings are even more critical to prevent wall failure from wave and surge impact.

**Enclosure Type:** Diagram 6 applies whether the enclosure is solid walls, lattice panels, or a combination. The key is that there is some obstruction between grade and the building's lowest floor. If the obstruction is minimal or purely decorative (very widely spaced lattice that clearly allows water flow), the building may be Diagram 5 instead.

**Enclosed Garage:** An attached garage with a roll-up or slider door is typically Diagram 6. The walls and door enclose the space below the main structure, and the opening must meet flood-opening requirements (the door opening typically counts toward the required area).

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the bottom of the building's first floor deck, not the floor of the enclosed space below. The enclosed space must be documented separately, and the flood openings must be verified for compliance.

---

## Diagram 7: Elevated on Full-Story Foundation Walls with Partial or Full Enclosure

**What It Represents:** Buildings supported on one or more complete stories of foundation walls (typically masonry or reinforced concrete) that are fully or partially enclosed. The space above the foundation walls becomes the lowest floor; the foundation walls and any space they enclose are treated as support structure, not living space.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the floor sitting atop the foundation walls—the lowest floor of the building proper.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** Same—the lowest enclosed living floor.

**Key Characteristics:**

- The building sits on one or more complete stories of reinforced masonry or concrete walls
- The walls are designed to withstand flood loads and hydrostatic pressure
- The space enclosed by the walls (if any) must have flood openings, or the walls must be designed as wet-floodable
- Unlike piers or columns, full-story foundation walls provide substantial structural support and can resist pressure

**Common Examples:** Historic masonry buildings, buildings in high-hazard flood areas where V zones require substantial structural support, or buildings with full-height basement-like foundations that are designed to be flood-resistant.

**Design Considerations:** In V zones, full-story foundation walls must be designed to withstand wave action and must include adequate openings or breakaway provisions for lower enclosures. The design must be certified by a professional engineer familiar with coastal flood engineering.

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the floor surface atop the foundation walls. The foundation walls themselves extend below, but their elevation is structural detail, not the lowest floor elevation.

---

## Diagram 8: Elevated on Crawlspace

**What It Represents:** Buildings elevated above grade on continuous or perimeter foundation walls that create a shallow crawlspace (typically 1–3 feet tall). The crawlspace is an enclosed or semi-enclosed space beneath the building's lowest occupied floor.

**A Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** The elevation of the building's lowest occupied floor (the first floor deck/rim joist), not the crawlspace floor.

**V Zone Lowest Floor Elevation:** Same—the lowest occupied floor, which is above the crawlspace.

**Key Characteristics:**

- Foundation walls create a shallow crawlspace
- The crawlspace contains mechanical and electrical systems (HVAC, electrical panel, water heater, plumbing)
- The crawlspace is typically not finished and is accessed via a small opening or trap door
- Flood openings or vents must allow water to flow into the crawlspace; utilities within must be designed to function in a wet-floodable environment (or elevated above expected flood levels)
- For compliance in A zones, FEMA allows crawlspaces because they are designed as wet-floodable (water is expected to enter, and systems are designed to tolerate submersion or are elevated within the crawlspace)

**Flood Opening Requirements (A Zones):** Crawlspaces must have openings (typically flood vents or automatic vents) totaling at least 1 square foot per 150 square feet of crawlspace floor area, on at least two sides. Vents must be accessible and free of obstruction.

**Utility Elevation in Crawlspaces:** All mechanical, electrical, and plumbing equipment must either be elevated above the 100-year flood elevation (preferably 1–2 feet above the anticipated peak water level) or be designed to function when submerged. Equipment sitting on the crawlspace floor will be damaged if floodwater enters. Modern codes require elevation of HVAC systems, electrical panels, and water heaters into the first-floor living space or into elevated platforms within the crawlspace.

**Crawlspace Floor (Section C, Elevation c):** The EC requires documentation of the "lowest horizontal structural member," which in a crawlspace building is typically the rim joist or floor system. This elevation is documented separately from the crawlspace floor because the structural floor, not the ground or crawlspace bottom, is the lowest floor of the building.

**As-Built Guidance:** The lowest floor elevation is the bottom of the building's first-floor structural system (rim joist, first floor framing). The crawlspace floor or grade beneath is secondary information used to verify the building is elevated.

---

## Diagram 9: For Section E and Section H

**What It Represents:** Diagram 9 is a simplified diagram used when Section E (no-survey buildings in AO/A without BFE/AR zones) or Section H (First Floor Height insurance documentation) is completed. It is not a structural type but a generic reference shape for property owners documenting building height above grade.

**Use:** When completing Section E (property owner measurement in Zone AO or similar), the diagram is simplified to show the building profile and the measurement point (distance from grade to lowest floor). It does not require the level of detail demanded for Diagrams 1–8.

## "As Built" Lowest Floor Guide and Figure 1

FEMA's "As Built" Lowest Floor Guide provides detailed illustrations for determining lowest floor elevation in different building types. The guide contains Figure 1, which summarizes the lowest floor determination:

**Key Principles from the Guide:**

1. **Lowest floor is the lowest enclosed living area.** For basements, it is the basement floor. For elevated buildings, it is the first-floor level of the living space.

2. **Lowest structural member in A zones:** If a building is elevated on piers with a crawlspace, the "lowest horizontal structural member" is documented separately from the lowest floor. This is the rim joist or lowest structural component of the first floor. This detail matters for Diagram 5, 6, 7, and 8 buildings in A zones, where utilities may occupy the crawlspace.

3. **V Zone rules differ from A Zone rules:** In V zones, basements and enclosures below the lowest living floor are not permitted (or only as unenclosed, wet-floodable space). The lowest floor must be the bottom of the lowest enclosed living space.

4. **Flood openings and enclosures:** Any enclosed space below the lowest floor must have adequate openings for flood water to flow freely. The presence or absence of proper openings affects whether the building meets code and may affect insurance rating.

## Notes from FEMA Guidance (Summary)

The FEMA bulletin referenced in EC documentation provides four critical notes:

**Note 1 — V Zone Elevations:** In V zones, the lowest floor must be elevated to the Design Flood Elevation (typically BFE plus 1–2 feet), and the entire building must be designed for V zone loads (wave action, impact, scour). Enclosed space below the lowest floor is severely restricted.

**Note 2 — A Zone Enclosures and Openings:** In A zones, enclosed space below the lowest floor (crawlspaces, partial enclosures) is permitted if it has adequate flood openings and is designed as wet-floodable. The openings must total 1 square foot per 150 square feet minimum, on at least two sides.

**Note 3 — V Zone Breakaway Walls:** If a V zone building has enclosure walls below the design flood elevation, those walls must be designed to break away cleanly when water pressure reaches expected levels, allowing water to flow through without damaging the building structure above. This is a complex engineering requirement and must be certified by a design professional.

**Note 4 — Crawlspace Construction (A Zones):** Crawlspaces are an acceptable above-grade foundation type in A zones if they are designed as wet-floodable. All utilities and equipment must be elevated or designed to tolerate submersion. Crawlspaces themselves do not count as living space and do not extend the "lowest floor" elevation upward.

## Selecting the Correct Diagram: A Decision Tree

To select the correct diagram:

1. **Is the building on a slab-on-grade (concrete poured directly on soil)?**
   - Yes, and no basement or enclosure below: **Diagram 1A** (with garage) or **1B** (no garage)
   - Yes, but with multiple levels at different slab heights: **Diagram 3**

2. **Does the building have a basement (enclosed, below-grade space)?**
   - Yes: **Diagram 2A** (with garage) or **2B** (no garage)
   - Yes, but also split levels: **Diagram 4**

3. **Is the building elevated on discrete supports (piers, posts, columns) with open space below?**
   - Yes, and the space below is completely unenclosed: **Diagram 5**
   - Yes, but the space is enclosed or partially enclosed: **Diagram 6**

4. **Is the building elevated on one or more stories of full foundation walls?**
   - Yes: **Diagram 7**

5. **Is the building elevated on a shallow crawlspace?**
   - Yes: **Diagram 8**

6. **Is this Section E or Section H only (no survey)?**
   - Yes: **Diagram 9**

Selecting the correct diagram ensures that the elevation measurements captured in the EC are interpreted correctly and produce usable results for insurance, compliance, and regulatory purposes.

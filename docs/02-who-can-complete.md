# Who Can Complete an Elevation Certificate?

## The Authorization Framework

The authority to complete different sections of an Elevation Certificate is defined by FEMA regulations and, critically, by **state law governing professional licensure**. Understanding who is authorized to certify what is essential both for property owners seeking services and for professionals determining their scope of work.

The FEMA Form FF-206-FY-22-152 is divided into several distinct sections, each with different authorization requirements. Some sections require professional certification by a licensed surveyor, engineer, or architect. Others can be completed by property owners or local officials. This structure reflects FEMA's recognition that not all elevation information requires the same level of technical oversight.

---

## Section C: Survey-Based Building Elevation Information

**Authorization:** Must be signed and sealed by a professional licensed under state law to certify elevation information.

Section C is where detailed elevation measurements are documented. This section captures the elevations of the building's lowest floor, next higher floor, lowest horizontal structural member, garage slab, mechanical and electrical equipment, and other critical points. These measurements are the foundation for determining First Floor Height and compliance with elevation standards.

Because Section C requires actual measured data and professional judgment about what constitutes the "lowest floor" (a determination that often involves interpreting building construction, drainage details, and regulatory requirements), **only professionals authorized by state law can certify this information**. In most states, this includes:

- **Licensed Land Surveyors** — The traditional professionals responsible for elevations and positioning
- **Licensed Professional Engineers** — Including civil engineers and structural engineers, provided they are licensed in the state and the state law permits them to certify elevations
- **Licensed Architects** — In states where the architecture license authorizes elevation certification

The critical qualifier is **state law**. Federal regulation does not determine who can sign Section C; state licensure law does. An engineer licensed in one state may not be authorized to certify elevations in another. A surveyor in California may have different authority than a surveyor in Florida. Before engaging a professional to complete an EC, verify that:

1. The professional holds a valid license in the relevant state
2. The state license explicitly authorizes elevation certification
3. The professional carries errors and omissions insurance
4. The professional is familiar with FEMA guidance and local floodplain requirements

## Section E: Building Measurement Information (No Survey)

**Authorization:** Property owner or any person authorized by state law; no survey or professional certification required.

Section E is the alternative to Section C for properties in specific flood zones where a detailed survey-based elevation is not needed. Section E applies to:

- **Zone AO** (areas of shallow flooding where flood depth, not elevation, is the controlling factor)
- **Zone AR or AR/AO** (areas that have been restored, where historical data may not apply)
- **Zone A without a mapped BFE** (areas where FEMA has not determined a specific base flood elevation, only that the area is at risk)

For these properties, FEMA recognizes that a detailed surveyed elevation may not alter insurance rating or regulatory outcome, so the barrier to entry is lower. **A property owner can complete Section E themselves**, documenting the building's construction details and elevation relationship using a tape measure, level, or even visual estimation where appropriate. No professional seal is required.

This provision serves a practical purpose: it allows owners of properties in lower-risk situations to obtain a usable EC without the expense of hiring a surveyor or engineer. However, property owners completing Section E should be honest about measurement accuracy. If measurements are visibly incorrect or internal inconsistencies suggest poor workmanship, the community or insurer may reject the EC and request a professionally prepared Section C instead.

---

## Section F: Property Owner Certification for Section E

**Authorization:** Property owner or authorized representative.

When a property owner or non-professional completes Section E, they must certify (sign) Section F, attesting that they provided truthful information to the best of their knowledge. This is not a professional seal; it is a statement of honesty. A false statement in Section F could constitute fraud, so property owners should take this certification seriously.

---

## Section G: Community Information

**Authorization:** Community floodplain manager or designated local official.

Section G captures information that only the local community can provide: permit history, as-built elevation data (if the community has it), the community's interpretation of the mapped BFE, community-specific minimum elevation standards, and any variances or waivers that apply to the property.

Many ECs submitted to communities are incomplete or missing Section G. The form's instructions are clear that the applicant should request the community to complete this section. In practice, some communities actively fill it out; others provide it only upon specific request; and some smaller communities lack the capacity to complete it thoroughly. If your community's floodplain manager hasn't completed Section G, ask directly—it strengthens the EC and ensures the community's official position is documented.

---

## Section H: First Floor Height (No Survey)

**Authorization:** Property owner, authorized representative, or local floodplain official; no survey required.

Section H is arguably the most important section for insurance rating under both zone-based and Risk Rating 2.0 methodologies. It documents the building's First Floor Height—the elevation of the lowest occupied floor—specifically for insurance purposes.

Notably, **Section H does not require a survey and does not require professional certification**. A property owner can measure their own First Floor Height using a tape measure and a simple level. Many insurance agents and property owners do exactly this for properties where Section C is impractical or unnecessary.

However, Section H also includes a checkbox for whether the height was "certified by a surveyor/engineer/architect." If a professional has measured and certified the elevation as part of Section C, that same professional's certification will inform Section H. The point is that **Section H can be completed with varying levels of rigor**, depending on the owner's resources and the insurer's requirements.

For highly accurate work, many professionals completing Section C will also prepare a carefully measured Section H, separating the detailed building elevation data (C) from the specific insurance-relevant measurement (H).

## Latitude and Longitude: An Important Clarification

The EC includes a field for latitude and longitude of the property. **Latitude and longitude do not need to come from a licensed surveyor.** The form includes a checkbox: "Latitude and Longitude determined by a licensed surveyor? Yes/No."

If the answer is "No," the property owner can use GPS, Google Maps, or any other source to determine coordinates. This flexibility recognizes that precise surveyed coordinates, while nice to have, are not essential to the EC's core purpose. What matters is that the property is correctly identified and that the elevations correspond to the correct structure.

---

## The Distinction Between Engineering Firms and Surveying Companies

A common source of confusion: must an Elevation Certificate be prepared by a surveying company?

**No.** The form can be completed by any professional—surveyor, engineer, or architect—authorized by state law to certify elevation information. Many structural engineering firms, civil engineering firms, and architectural firms prepare ECs regularly. They do not need to be surveying companies.

What matters is licensure and state authorization, not the professional's traditional specialty. A civil engineer licensed in Florida and authorized to certify elevations can prepare an EC. A structural engineer licensed in Georgia can prepare an EC if Georgia law permits it. A surveyor can prepare an EC anywhere their license is valid.

In practice, property owners should select the professional best suited to their project's scope. For a complex, fully elevated commercial structure, an engineer may be ideal. For a modest residential property in a B zone pursuing a LOMA, the property owner might handle Section H themselves. For survey-intensive work involving ties to benchmarks and detailed datum conversion, a surveyor's expertise is invaluable.

## Professional Liability and Responsibility

Any professional who signs and seals an Elevation Certificate is certifying the accuracy and completeness of the information in their signature sections. This certification carries professional liability. If an EC later proves to be inaccurate—measurements were wrong, the wrong building was documented, elevations were misidentified—the professional may be subject to:

- Claims by the property owner for economic loss (e.g., if the EC was rejected and they had to hire another professional)
- Claims by the insurer for overpayment of claims based on inaccurate elevation data
- Regulatory action from the state licensing board
- Civil litigation from downstream parties (subsequent owners, lenders, communities)

For this reason, professionals should:

- Maintain detailed field notes and photographs
- Follow the current FEMA guidance and their state's regulations
- Carry adequate errors and omissions insurance
- Perform quality control checks before submission
- Document the basis for elevation determinations and measurement methodology
- Be conservative in interpreting ambiguous situations (when in doubt, document the uncertainty in writing)

## Verification and Chain of Authority

When reviewing a completed Elevation Certificate, verify:

1. **Professional Licensure:** Check the signatory's license status through the state's professional licensing board. Invalid or expired licenses render the EC's professional portions uncertifiable.

2. **Appropriate Authorization:** Confirm that the state license held by the signatory actually authorizes elevation certification. Some states license engineers broadly; others restrict elevation certification to specific disciplines.

3. **Seal Validity:** Professional seals must be current and valid. An expired seal is not acceptable.

4. **Signature and Date:** The professional's signature and date must be present. Unsigned or undated documents are not executable.

5. **Consistency:** Check that the elevations documented in different sections (C, H) are consistent. If they differ significantly, ask why.

If any of these elements are missing or questionable, the community or insurer may reject the EC and request corrections or resubmission. Catching issues early is far preferable to discovering problems after formal submission.
